\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates how modern large language models collapse the traditional
``effort gap'' in reverse engineering. Two empirical case studies --- Spider
Farmer BLE devices and EcoFlow PowerOcean energy systems --- together with a
third recursive case study of the paper-generation process itself demonstrate
how AI-assisted analysis turns theoretical protocol knowledge into practical,
replicable local integrations. We then assess whether this collapse is a force
for interoperability and right-to-repair or a systemic security risk. To that
end we propose a research methodology that treats AI conversation transcripts,
vendor artifacts, and version history as first-class evidence. The dual-use,
\emph{sloppification}, and model-collapse risks of AI-assisted research are
made explicit and mapped to mitigations that the methodology operationalises.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Author's Note --- Advice for reading}
\label{sec:authors-note}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Author's Note --- Advice for reading}

\noindent A short methodological note from me (Florian Krebs), placed
up-front because \emph{how} you read this paper matters as much as
\emph{what} is in it. Treat the note as an invitation --- to read
closely, to argue back, and to bring evidence. The framings below are
the lenses I would most want a thoughtful reader to bring to the rest
of the document; none of them softens an empirical claim, and all of
them open a door to discussion.

\paragraph{From a horticulture controller to AI safety.}
This paper began as a pure interoperability project --- a few weekends
of trying to bring a Spider Farmer BLE controller and an EcoFlow
PowerOcean energy system into Home Assistant without depending on the
vendor cloud. AI assistance turned what would have been months of solo
reverse engineering into days, and the integrations work. But the
\emph{speed} of that collapse --- the disappearance of the effort gap
that has historically protected proprietary protocols --- kept
surfacing harder questions: if I can do this in days as a hobbyist,
what does the same compression look like for an attacker, a paper
mill, or an unverified claim that nobody noticed an LLM wrote? The
case studies stayed; the centre of gravity shifted. What started as
\emph{``how do I make this controller talk to my smart home''} became
\emph{``what does responsible practice look like when the methodology
that produced this paper is the same methodology that, used badly,
fills the literature with synthetic noise
(\cref{sec:disc-sloppification},
\cref{sec:disc-model-collapse})?''} That arc is the reason the paper
has a \cref{sec:pandora} at all; without it, this would have been a
how-to and not a piece of empirical methodology.

\paragraph{Process, not product.}
View this work less as the \emph{output} of frontier agentic AI and
more as a \emph{process}. The methodology here tries to leverage
generative AI's analytical lift while warding against its documented
failure modes --- fabrication, opacity, dual-use amplification, and the
asymmetric collapse developed in \cref{sec:disc-asymmetry} and
\cref{sec:disc-model-collapse}. The case-study substance is mostly
prior community work; the contribution is the eight-practice register
in \cref{sec:pandora} and the discipline that produced it. If the
discipline is the contribution, then disagreement \emph{about} the
discipline is the most useful kind of engagement a reader can offer,
and it is the kind I am most hoping for.

\paragraph{Frameworks evolve with capabilities.}
The eight practices in \cref{sec:pandora} are calibrated to a specific
snapshot --- Claude Opus 4.7, the Claude Code CLI, the Consensus
literature front end, and consumer-IoT case studies in mid-2026. That
snapshot will age. Some practices will be subsumed by tooling
(transcript export becomes a click); some will become inadequate
(verification-status legends will need finer grain); some I cannot yet
name will need to be invented. Treat the eight as a starting point and
a conversation opener, not a final state, and please tell me when you
see one of the eight begin to age out.

\paragraph{Pandora's jar is open.}
The shattered-jar logo in the front matter is deliberate, and the
framing is firm: the empirical literature on fabricated citations
(\texttt{docs/sources.md} L-SLOP-1, L-SLOP-2), paper-mill output
(L-SLOP-8), and longer-horizon model collapse (L-MC-1, L-MC-3)
documents what is already out of the jar, and no appeal to obscurity,
vendor terms of service, or moratoria on capability research will put
it back. The intact-jar reference in \cref{sec:pandora} is the
Hesiodic counterpoint: the jar can be closed only if Hope is what
remains inside, and Hope here is operational --- structured,
transparent, attributed practice that the next reader can audit,
replicate, or refute. The point of the framing is not pessimism; it is
to name what we are working with so the work has somewhere honest to
go.

\paragraph{The irony, acknowledged.}
I am aware of the obvious irony of writing a paper \emph{about} the
failure modes of AI-assisted research \emph{using} AI-assisted
research. I do not have a clever way out of it, and pretending I did
would itself be one of the failure modes. The honest answer is the one
operationalised in \cref{sec:ai-disclosure}: AI usage is disclosed at
the \emph{artifact} level, every claim is backed by an exportable
transcript committed in this repository, and the methodology tries to
make the discipline that --- absent disclosure --- would let the
failure modes scale. Refusing to use the tools while writing about
them would not have produced a more honest paper; it would have ceded
the methodological ground to those who use them without disclosure
(\cref{sec:disc-sloppification}, \cref{sec:disc-model-collapse}).
Pandora's jar is open. The only way out is through, and ``through''
looks like this --- disclosed, attributed, auditable, and willing to
be argued with.

\paragraph{On the unsaid accusation of cheating.}
A second strand of skepticism, often unsaid, is the suspicion that
AI-assisted authorship is a quieter form of plagiarism --- that the
methodology I am defending here is, on closer reading, an elaborate way
to launder synthetic prose into the scientific record. I take that
suspicion seriously and I think it is largely correct \emph{about the
prevailing baseline} (\cref{sec:disc-sloppification} documents what
undisclosed AI use looks like at scale; the empirical fabrication-rate
literature in \cref{sec:ai-disclosure-disclaimers} is what concealment
produces). What I am trying to construct in this paper is the
alternative the suspicion does not yet have: a discipline under which
AI-assisted research is inspectable on its own terms, transcript by
transcript, citation by verified citation. The eight practices
in~\cref{sec:pandora} are framed
in~\cref{sec:ai-skeptic-integration} as exactly this --- a
\emph{condition catalogue} that converts a legitimate objection into a
checkable one. If you arrived as a skeptic, the most useful thing you
can do with this paper is to argue with the catalogue: which row is
missing, which row is insufficient, which row will the next tooling
cycle subsume. That is the conversation I am writing for.

\paragraph{What surprised me about the assistant.}
Working through the case studies, I expected --- and braced for ---
moments where the model would refuse to help me reverse-engineer a
proprietary BLE protocol or pull credentials out of a vendored APK,
or where the session would be flagged or terminated. That happened
far less often than I anticipated. The dominant register was
\emph{cooperative competence}, including for steps that an attacker
would also want. This is a useful empirical observation, and a
sobering one: it tells us that current guardrails are real but
bounded, and that the surface they protect is much smaller than the
surface they touch. The conclusion I take from this is uncomfortable
but not pessimistic: guardrails are a band-aid --- necessary, but a
band-aid (\cref{sec:disc-dual-use} develops this against the Mythos /
Project Glasswing case). The durable answer is \emph{security by
design at the artifact level} --- published specifications,
authenticated and scoped APIs, and threat models that assume the
existence of capable, willing, AI-augmented adversaries
(\cref{fig:dual-use} and \cref{fig:threat-models}). Treating AI as
just another vector under-counts what is changing; the magnitude and
frequency of attempts that any given system will face is materially
higher in 2026 than it was in 2024, and the trajectory points further
the same way. Build for that.

\paragraph{Open to constructive criticism --- actively.}
I welcome criticism, replication attempts, and challenges to the
framework --- to the eight practices, the case-study choices, and the
legal framing in \cref{sec:ai-disclosure}. Disagree with the
synthesis; surface a counter-example; flag a fabricated citation, an
unsourced legal claim, or a verification-status entry that cannot
survive scrutiny --- all of these are useful, and all of them are
welcome. The transcript-as-artifact discipline (rule~4 in
\texttt{CLAUDE\_CODE\_INSTRUCTIONS.md}) is what makes serious critique
tractable: every claim in this paper is backed by an exportable
conversation log committed in this repository, so an objection can
land on a specific line, transcript, or commit rather than on vibes.
If you find such a claim that cannot survive scrutiny, or if you simply
have a better way to do any part of this, I genuinely want to hear it.

\paragraph{Not a paper mill --- a shower-thought-to-plausibility loop.}
This needs saying clearly because the empirical baseline is bad:
paper-mill output and undisclosed AI-assisted production are the
dominant failure modes (\texttt{docs/sources.md} L-SLOP-8, L-SLOP-1,
L-SLOP-2). The methodology described here is designed to do the
opposite. The agent pipeline (\texttt{docs/prompts/}) takes a
\emph{hypothesis} --- often a ``shower thought'' provoked by a specific
device, transcript, or paragraph --- and iterates it through structured
passes (research $\rightarrow$ source analysis $\rightarrow$ scientific
writing $\rightarrow$ illustration $\rightarrow$ layout / readability
scrutiny). At each stage the question is the same:
\emph{does this still look plausible against the evidence we now have?}
Plausible hypotheses survive into the paper with their verification
status visible; implausible ones are rejected, recorded in the logbook
with a reason, and remain auditable. The output volume is therefore
intentionally bounded by what survives plausibility, not by what an
LLM can be cajoled into producing. The repository carries roughly as
many lines of methodological scaffolding as paper prose precisely so
the volume cannot run away from the verification.

\paragraph{An invitation, in concrete form.}
GitHub issues on this repository are a first-class channel for new
ideas, counter-examples, and critique. Issues labelled \texttt{idea}
(a hypothesis worth investigating), \texttt{critique} (a specific
challenge to a claim, figure, or framing), or \texttt{provenance-gap}
(a missing transcript or unverifiable artifact) feed directly into the
agent pipeline. An \texttt{idea} triggers a research-protocol pass; a
\texttt{critique} routes to the relevant scrutinizer hand-back; a
\texttt{provenance-gap} routes to the meta-process case study
(\cref{sec:case-meta}). If you have
read this far, you are already the kind of reader I am writing for ---
and the issue tracker is the best place to continue the conversation.

\section*{Executive Summary}
\label{sec:executive-summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Executive Summary}

\noindent\emph{This summary re-presents the claims of the full paper for
readers deciding whether to read further. It introduces no new findings;
every numerical anchor below is sourced and developed in the body.}

\paragraph{Research question.} Is AI-assisted hacking primarily a means to
unlock interoperability, or does it instead magnify security risk by making
obscurity ineffective? The paper answers: both --- but asymmetrically, and
the asymmetry favours integrators over attackers.

\paragraph{Software-side evidence that the effort gap has collapsed.}
Peer-reviewed measurements anchor the software-side compression: a 24.4\%
reduction in cognitive burden when reading AI-augmented decompiler output
[L-RE-2]; a 31\%~$\rightarrow$~53\% correct-solve-rate uplift on
reverse-engineering tasks (with the authors' own $p = 0.189$ caveat
preserved at the body) [L-RE-3]; and a $>$100\% improvement in
re-executability for AI-assisted code synthesis [L-RE-1]. The two case
studies developed below report the same compression at workflow level
(\cref{sec:sf-kpis}, \cref{sec:ef-kpis}).

\paragraph{Hardware-side evidence that the same collapse is underway.}
The hardware side rests on a structurally different evidence base. An
exposed UART suffices for firmware extraction in more than 45\% of 24
commercial IoT devices~\citep{vasile2018breakingallthethings};
intermediate hardware-reverse-engineering proficiency is reachable in
roughly 14 weeks of structured training~\citep{becker2020hwreexploratory};
and the practitioner-handbook bookends move from the Grand / Huang
2003--2004 baseline (bespoke equipment, expert-only skill floor) to van
Woudenberg \& O'Flynn (2022, single volume plus a roughly USD~300
toolchain). The asymmetry between the two evidence bases is itself a
finding, treated as \cref{sec:synthesis-evidence-asymmetry}.

\paragraph{Asymmetric collapse.} The same compression that benefits
integrators benefits attackers, but not equally. Recent evaluations report
that LLMs are far more cooperative than competent at automated exploit
generation --- no model successfully synthesised exploits for refactored
security labs [L-VD-1] --- while disclosed-CVE proof-of-concept generation
reaches 8--34\% on public information alone and 68--72\% with adaptive
reasoning [L-VD-2]. The cost-asymmetry literature reports that
LLM-mediated malicious-content production is roughly 125--500$\times$
cheaper than human effort [L-VD-5]\footnote{The L-VD-1 refactored-labs
cornerstone and the L-VD-5 cost-asymmetry quantitative anchor are recorded
at \texttt{[edge-case]} verification status in \texttt{docs/sources.md}
(load-bearing, first-of-its-kind, awaiting human \texttt{[lit-read]}). They
are footnoted here per the verification ladder (CLAUDE.md, 2026-05-02
extension) and not promoted to inline citation.}. The defender-side
corollary is blunt: a static-token study of 20 production weather apps
concludes that ``it is not possible to securely hide sensitive information
within applications'' [L-HC-4].

\paragraph{Two case studies.} Spider Farmer (BLE horticulture controllers)
was sealed by AES-128-CBC with hardcoded keys and a self-signed-cert MQTT
broker; AI-assisted reconciliation across four independent
reverse-engineering attempts elucidated the protocol surface and shipped
as a working Home Assistant integration in roughly 10.5~h of AI-assisted
work versus an estimated 60--120~h of manual baseline
(\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer}). EcoFlow PowerOcean (residential energy
systems) was sealed by an undocumented legacy REST endpoint and a
published-but-unused Open API; AI-assisted three-surface reconciliation
produced a local-control integration and surfaced a previously
undocumented attack surface that is enumerated under the redaction policy
(\cref{sec:case-ecoflow}). Both cases illustrate the dual-use signature
explicitly: the same workflow yields both the local integration and the
recovered live attack surface.

\paragraph{Eight integrated practices.} The methodological response ---
see \cref{fig:eight-practices} --- is the integration of eight
individually unoriginal practices: transcript preservation,
verification-status labelling, provenance maps, mirror discipline, the
recursive meta-process case study, base-rate-anchored AI disclosure, legal
honesty about authorship, and FAIR alignment as a precondition. The
novelty is the \emph{integration}, not any single row; details are in
\cref{sec:pandora}.

\paragraph{Headline finding.} Obscurity is dead as a primary defence for
consumer IoT: the labour-cost moat that protected proprietary protocols
has been compressed by an order of magnitude on the integration side and
is being compressed, more unevenly, on the offensive side. The
interoperability dividend is real; so is the dual-use surface; the eight
practices reduce the methodological externalities of AI-assisted security
research without requiring the underlying protocols to remain secret.
Readers wanting the contributions should turn to \cref{sec:contributions};
the evidence-asymmetry meta-finding is in
\cref{sec:synthesis-evidence-asymmetry}; the prescriptive register is
\cref{sec:pandora}.

\section{Introduction and Motivation}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{The effort gap as a security model}
\label{sec:effort-gap}
The dominant security posture for consumer IoT is not cryptographic but
economic: proprietary protocols, obfuscated APKs, and undocumented APIs raise
the activation energy of integration high enough that a casual researcher
gives up. We name this implicit defence the \emph{effort gap} and argue that
it has functioned as the de-facto interoperability barrier of the last decade.

\subsection{Research question}
\label{sec:research-question}
\begin{quote}
Is AI-assisted hacking primarily a means to unlock interoperability, or does
it instead magnify security risk by making obscurity ineffective?
\end{quote}

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{User sovereignty.} Owners of energy and horticulture hardware
        are increasingly forced into vendor cloud silos for basic local
        control.
  \item \textbf{Privacy and data sovereignty.} Cloud-bound consumer IoT
        routinely exports telemetry that users did not bargain for.
        Ren et al.\ (2019, \emph{IMC}) characterised 81 devices in
        34{,}586 controlled experiments and documented unencrypted
        exposures and traffic to third-party
        destinations~\citep{ren2019iotinforming}; Nan et al.\ (2023)
        statically analysed \textbf{6{,}208} IoT companion apps and
        found \textbf{1{,}973} of them --- covering $\geq\,1{,}559$
        distinct vendors --- exposing user data without proper
        disclosure~\citep{nan2023iotapp}; Apthorpe et al.\ (2017) showed
        that even \emph{encrypted} IoT traffic leaks user activity to a
        passive observer [L-PRIV-2]. A local Home Assistant integration
        replaces the vendor-cloud round-trip with on-LAN BLE or HTTPS,
        letting the owner keep using the device as it was sold while
        exercising the data-minimisation principle that GDPR Art.\ 5(1)(c)
        requires of controllers but rarely sees enforced [L-PRIV-10];
        Kazlouski et al.\ (2022) provide a concrete existence proof ---
        Fitbit core functionality is preserved when third-party domains
        are blocked at the network layer [L-PRIV-9].
  \item \textbf{Right-to-repair and \S{} 69e UrhG / EU 2009/24/EC.} European
        law explicitly permits decompilation for interoperability; AI
        assistance changes the \emph{practical} --- not the \emph{legal} ---
        accessibility of that right. Legal framing to be sourced; see
        \texttt{docs/sources.md} entries S-EF-9, S-EF-10.
  \item \textbf{Dual use.} The same workflow that yields a Home Assistant
        integration yields recovered MQTT credentials and live device write
        paths. The Spider Farmer case study contains a concrete instance of
        recovered live credentials (see \cref{sec:sf-security}).
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility crisis in security research.} Published
        ``hacks'' rarely ship with prompts, transcripts, or commit-pinned
        artifacts. We argue this is now a methodological defect, not a
        stylistic choice.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Thesis and contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}
We claim that the effort gap has \emph{collapsed asymmetrically}: it has
collapsed faster for interoperability work (where success is verifiable
against a working integration) than for offensive work (which still requires
target selection, persistence, and operational tradecraft). The
activation-energy framing is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:boredom-barrier}:
the thermodynamic endpoint --- a working integration --- is unchanged, but
AI-assisted analysis lowers the barrier separating theory from practice.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig2-boredom-barrier.pdf}
  \Description{Reaction-coordinate diagram showing two energy curves with identical endpoints; the AI-assisted curve has a lower activation-energy peak than the manual baseline.}
  \caption{\textbf{The boredom barrier.} Reaction-coordinate view of a reverse-engineering project. The thermodynamic endpoint --- a working integration --- is unchanged. AI-assisted analysis lowers the activation energy $E_\mathrm{a}$ by absorbing the rote, low-novelty work where most projects historically stalled.}
  \label{fig:boredom-barrier}
\end{figure}

The effort-gap collapse documented by this paper is software-side: it
concerns the analysis of mobile companion apps, cloud APIs, and BLE / MQTT
protocols where the obscurity barrier is a labour cost imposed on a
researcher who already has root-equivalent access to the artefacts. A
parallel, \emph{researcher-hypothesised and AI-assisted-research-confirmed}
extension of the same thesis applies to the \textbf{hardware-side
effort gap} --- the cost of physically extracting firmware, locating
debug interfaces, and mounting side-channel or fault-injection attacks
on commodity embedded devices.

Two peer-reviewed quantitative anchors triangulate this hypothesis. The
ChipWhisperer platform documents an approximately 100$\times$
equipment-cost reduction relative to prior commercial side-channel
rigs (L-HW-RE-2)\footnote{Cluster A.2 entry L-HW-RE-2 (ChipWhisperer
2014) and the L-HW-RE-5 practitioner handbook cluster remain
catalogued in \texttt{docs/sources.md} at grey-literature /
pending-confirmation status: the load-bearing $\sim$100$\times$
equipment-cost quote could not be verbatim-retrieved from the 2014
paper in the Source Analyzer pass (all open-access mirrors returned
HTTP~403), and the L-HW-RE-5 books are practitioner / grey literature
rather than peer-reviewed sources; they are therefore referenced via
this footnote rather than as inline citations, per the verification
ladder (\texttt{CLAUDE.md}, 2026-05-02
extension).\label{fn:hwre-cluster}}. Vasile, Oswald \& Chothia's
systematic survey of 24 commercial IoT devices reports that an
exposed UART suffices for firmware extraction in more than 45\% of
cases~\citep{vasile2018breakingallthethings}.

A SOUPS 2020 cognitive-process study finds that students reach
intermediate hardware-reverse-engineering proficiency in 14 weeks of
structured training, quantifying skill-floor
compression~\citep{becker2020hwreexploratory}. A peer-reviewed
embedded-systems attack taxonomy (Papp, Ma \& Butty{\'a}n,
2015)~\citep{papp2015embedded} complements the cost- and skill-floor
anchors at the categorisation layer.

Practitioner handbooks bookend the period: the 2003--2004 Grand /
Huang baseline (bespoke equipment, multi-day soldering, expert-only
skill floor) and the 2022 van Woudenberg \& O'Flynn handbook (single
volume + $\sim$USD 300 toolchain)
(L-HW-RE-5)\textsuperscript{\ref{fn:hwre-cluster}}. AI-assisted PCB
component identification has matured; full netlist reconstruction is
emerging~\citep{botero2021hwretutorial}; and JTAGulator-class
commodity devices, documented in grey literature, automate
debug-interface discovery on unknown PCBs~\citep{grand2013jtagulator}.

The hardware-side cluster is presented as a \emph{triangulated
practitioner observation}, not as a benchmarked finding equivalent to
the software-side anchors; the asymmetry between the two evidence
bases is itself a finding of this paper and is treated explicitly in
\cref{sec:synthesis-evidence-asymmetry}.

The paper contributes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A definition of the effort gap and an operationalisation as
        measurable KPIs (\texttt{docs/methodology.md} \S 10), complementing
        prior effort-gap exemplars in the reverse-engineering literature
        (L-RE-2).
  \item Two empirical case studies with complete artifact provenance
        (\texttt{experiments/spider-farmer/},
        \texttt{experiments/ecoflow-powerocean/}), against the consumer-IoT
        base rate of \textgreater{}70\% of 17{,}243 BLE-enabled Android APKs
        carrying at least one vulnerability (L-BLE-4).
  \item An auditable methodology that treats AI conversation transcripts as
        research artifacts, mapped to git history and vendor code
        (\texttt{experiments/*/provenance.md}); this complements --- and
        operationalises at artifact level --- the publishing-system reforms
        proposed in the Stockholm Declaration (L-SLOP-7), the
        practitioner-level ethical-use guidance of Cheng, Calhoun \&
        Reedy (L-SLOP-10), and the AI-detector / citation /
        image-verification landscape reviewed by Pellegrina \& Helmy
        (L-SLOP-12).
  \item A synthesis of interoperability gains versus dual-use risks, grounded
        in the verified evidence of both cases.
  \item A meta-observation on the \emph{evidence asymmetry} between
        software-side and hardware-side effort-gap compression, framed
        as a finding about the maturity of empirical
        hardware-security-research methodology rather than as a
        weakness of the cluster A.2 hypothesis
        (\cref{sec:synthesis-evidence-asymmetry}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope and non-goals}
\label{sec:scope}
\label{sec:scope-non-goals}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We do \textbf{not} publish weaponised exploit code; recovered
        credentials are flagged for redaction (\texttt{docs/logbook.md}
        2026-05-01 audit entry).
  \item We do \textbf{not} offer legal advice; AI-generated legal opinions

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

The full operational methodology is maintained in
\texttt{docs/methodology.md}. This section summarises it for the paper and
pins the design decisions that distinguish the work.

\subsection{Unit of analysis: the AI-assisted research workflow}
\label{sec:unit-of-analysis}
A \emph{workflow} is the tuple (vendor artifacts, AI conversation
transcripts, integration code, git history, validation evidence). The case
study, not the device or the model, is the experimental unit.

\subsection{Artifact collection}
\label{sec:artifact-collection}
For each case we vendor:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Vendor binaries and documentation (APKs, PDFs, firmware where
        lawful).
  \item AI conversation transcripts
        (\texttt{raw\_conversations (copy\&paste, web)/}).
  \item Community reference implementations, embedded as plain files for
        direct citation (repo commit \texttt{ffdf60c}).
  \item The integration code under study, embedded under
        \texttt{original/} rather than referenced as a moving submodule.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Provenance mapping}
\label{sec:provenance-mapping}
Every technical claim is mapped, in a per-case
\texttt{provenance.md}, to (a) the transcript that proposed it, (b) the file
and line numbers in \texttt{original/} that confirm it, and (c) the commit
SHA at which the mapping was verified. Source-register entries carry a
verification status --- \texttt{[repo-vendored]},
\texttt{[repo-referenced]}, \texttt{[unverified-external]},
\texttt{[lit-retrieved]}, \texttt{[lit-read]}, or \texttt{[needs-research]}
--- so the paper can be honest about what has and has not been independently
checked.

\subsection{AI transparency and \emph{lege artis}}
\label{sec:lege-artis}
Following the DFG Guidelines for Safeguarding Good Research
Practice~\citep{dfg2023}, every section discloses where AI assistance was
used and where it was rejected, corrected, or contradicted by the underlying
code. AI-generated legal analysis is never used as legal advice. The
repository's AI policy is canonicalised in
\texttt{CLAUDE\_CODE\_INSTRUCTIONS.md} and aliased in
\texttt{.instructions.md} and \texttt{copilot-instructions.md}.

\subsection{KPIs (effort-gap operationalisation)}
\label{sec:kpis}
Drawn from \texttt{docs/methodology.md} \S 10 and applied per case study:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Process:} artifact-acquisition completeness, prompt-iteration
        count, automation ratio.
  \item \textbf{Experimental success:} probe success rate, functional
        coverage, reproducibility score.
  \item \textbf{Time and effort:} time-to-first-working-integration,
        effort-gap metric (estimated manual hours vs.\ AI-assisted hours).
  \item \textbf{Problem characterisation:} discovery density, candidate-space
        size, obscurity depth.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Validation and reproducibility}
\label{sec:validation}
Each finding is reproduced against the embedded \texttt{original/} code at a
pinned commit. Failed attempts are recorded in the logbook and (where
applicable) in the per-case \texttt{provenance.md}. The KPI table at the end
of each case study reports verified, not aspirational, numbers.

\subsection{Ethics}
\label{sec:ethics}
The dual-use evaluation is part of the methodology, not a postscript. For
each case we explicitly enumerate (a) what becomes easier for an integrator
and (b) what becomes easier for an attacker. Live credentials, device
serials, and broker IPs are flagged for redaction before any public release

\subsection{System and threat model}
\label{sec:meta-threat-model}
The unit of analysis here is recursive: the paper that documents AI-assisted
reverse engineering is itself an AI-assisted artifact. The ``system'' is the
paper-generation pipeline: prompts, AI conversation transcripts, repository
state, and the human researcher in the loop. Specific risks to evaluate are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fabricated citations.} LLMs are known to hallucinate
        references; an entry surfaced from a database is not the same as a
        paper that has been read.
  \item \textbf{Silent memorisation / plagiarism.} AI-drafted prose may
        reproduce training-data text without attribution.
  \item \textbf{Prompt injection.} Vendor APK strings, community-thread
        excerpts, and PDF contents embedded under
        \texttt{experiments/*/original/} are read by the agent and could in
        principle inject instructions.
  \item \textbf{Misrepresentation of source claims.} A Consensus-surfaced
        abstract is not a peer-reviewed claim until the full text has been
        read.
  \item \textbf{Tooling drift.} Identical prompts in later sessions produce
        non-identical outputs.
  \item \textbf{AI-generated legal opinion mistaken for sourced legal
        commentary.} A specific instance from the EcoFlow transcripts has
        already been flagged; the failure mode is generalisable.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Findings --- security and dual-use implications}
\label{sec:meta-security}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fabricated citations.} All $\sim$50 entries in the
        literature register are \texttt{[lit-retrieved]} only. Any upgrade
        to \texttt{[lit-read]} without the researcher actually reading the
        paper would be a fabrication. The empirical base rates make this
        concrete: Walters \& Wilder (2023) found \textbf{55\% of
        ChatGPT-3.5 and 18\% of GPT-4 generated citations were
        fabricated} in literature reviews, with a further 24--43\% of
        the \emph{real} citations carrying substantive
        errors~\citep{walters2023fabricated}. McGowan et al.\ (2023)
        found only \textbf{2 of 35} ChatGPT-generated psychiatry
        citations were real~\citep{mcgowan2023chatgpt}. Chelli et al.\
        (2024) measured hallucination rates of 28.6\%--91.4\% across
        LLMs in systematic-review reference
        generation~\citep{chelli2024hallucination}. These are not edge
        cases.
  \item \textbf{AI-generated legal analysis.} A specific opinion in
        transcript T3 line 147 (EcoFlow) is flagged in
        \texttt{docs/sources.md} and \cref{sec:disc-r2r} --- without that
        flag, it would have entered the paper as if sourced.
  \item \textbf{Live-credential leakage.} \texttt{docs/sources.md} S-SF-5
        carried the recovered MQTT credentials for the Spider Farmer
        broker. These have been replaced with \texttt{[REDACTED]} markers
        in all researcher-authored files per
        \texttt{docs/redaction-policy.md} R-SF-1..R-SF-2, and the raw
        values were excised from git history on 2026-05-04 by a
        \texttt{git filter-repo --replace-text} pass against the
        catalogue in \texttt{docs/git-history-rewrite-plan.md}; the
        rewrite tip carries the annotated tag
        \texttt{pre-publication-clean}. Vendored binary archives in
        \texttt{experiments/spider-farmer/original/doc/} retain the
        strings inside their packed entries because
        \texttt{--replace-text} does not descend into binary blobs; the
        policy treats them as a public-mirror cut-over decision (drop
        the archives or ship them with a caveat) rather than a residual
        leak.
  \item \textbf{Prompt injection from imported artifacts.} Vendor APK
        strings, PDF contents, and community-thread excerpts under
        \texttt{experiments/*/original/} are read by the AI agent. The
        mitigation is the AI policy's labelling requirement plus
        researcher verification of every AI-attributed claim.
  \item \textbf{Tooling drift.} The workflow and committed artifacts are
        the reproducible unit; the AI's exact tokens are not.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the AI workflow added}
\label{sec:synthesis-ai-lift}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Spider Farmer.} Not protocol \emph{discovery} --- the four
        community implementations had already done that --- but
        \emph{reconciliation} across conflicting key tables and verification
        of the dynamic-IV slice formula.
  \item \textbf{EcoFlow PowerOcean.} Surface enumeration and field-name
        convention recovery: a manual reverse-engineer would have eventually
        found the legacy endpoint, but the AI-assisted workflow recovered
        the full writeable-parameter catalogue from APK constants in a
        single pass.
  \item \textbf{Meta-process.} Skeleton generation from sparse evidence,
        structured literature retrieval at scale, and the disciplined
        application of verification-status labels to keep the paper honest
        about what it has and has not read.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Common patterns}
\label{sec:synthesis-patterns}
\begin{itemize}
  \item All three defences/processes relied on the \emph{combination} of
        obscurity and effort, not on either alone --- and in the
        meta-process, on the \emph{combination} of access control and
        labour, where the labour is the researcher reading and verifying.
  \item All three yielded clean outputs once a single organising piece of
        evidence (key table; API surface; verification-status legend) was
        identified.
  \item In all three cases, AI assistance compressed the \emph{organisation
        and reconciliation} phase far more than the \emph{discovery}
        phase. The effort-gap collapse is unevenly distributed across
        workflow stages.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limits of the comparison}
\label{sec:synthesis-limits}
Three cases is still small. The meta-process case is also recursive ---
the paper documenting AI-assisted reverse engineering is itself an
AI-assisted artifact, which means selection bias and tooling drift apply
to its analysis the same way they apply to the integration cases. We
treat the meta-process case as evidence for the methodology, not as
independent confirmation of the central thesis.

A second selection limit deserves explicit treatment. Both Spider
Farmer and EcoFlow PowerOcean are \emph{consumer-market} IoT products.
One could plausibly hypothesise that industrial or higher-end hardware
--- with regulated certification, longer component lifetimes, and
explicitly safety-driven security goals --- would not exhibit the same
effort-gap collapse. The literature offers a \emph{qualified} answer in
two parts:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The consumer-IoT base rate of vulnerability is
        empirically high, not anomalous.} Zhao et al.\ (2022) measured
        1{,}362{,}906 deployed IoT devices and found \textbf{28.25\%
        suffer from at least one N-days vulnerability}, with
        \textbf{88\% of analysed MQTT servers having no password
        protection}~\citep{zhao2022iotbaseline}. Kumar et al.\
        (2019) studied 83 million devices in 16 million households and
        documented widespread weak default credentials and open
        services (L-CONS-2). Davis et al.\ (2020) explicitly compared
        well-known and lesser-known vendors and found that
        lesser-known vendors are systematically less-regulated and
        less-scrutinised (L-CONS-3) --- a pattern that applies to both
        case studies in this paper. Sivakumaran et al.\ (2023) found
        \textbf{$>$70\% of 17{,}243 BLE-enabled Android APKs contain at
        least one security vulnerability} (L-BLE-4). Spider Farmer and
        EcoFlow are not exceptional cases of consumer-IoT insecurity;
        they are the median.
  \item \textbf{Industrial / higher-end hardware is \emph{not}
        automatically immune.} Serror et al.\ (2021) frame IIoT as
        sharing many security concerns with consumer IoT despite its
        different goal stack of safety, productivity, and longer
        lifetimes~\citep{serror2021iiot}. Duque Ant{\'o}n et al.\
        (2021) directly puncture the ``industrial-grade therefore
        safer'' intuition: they enumerated \textbf{$>$13{,}000 OT/ICS
        devices directly exposed on the public internet, almost all
        containing at least one
        vulnerability}~\citep{duqueanton2021ics}. Asghar et al.\
        (2019) observe that ICS were originally designed for isolated
        environments and that modern enterprise integration has opened
        structural cybersecurity problems that the original designs
        never anticipated~\citep{asghar2019ics}. Higher cost, regulatory floors, and
        certification raise the \emph{baseline} but do not close the
        effort gap that AI assistance compresses.
\end{itemize}
The honest conclusion is that the case selection over-samples
consumer-grade hardware, but the inference is not ``industrial would
not have these problems'' --- it is \emph{narrower}: the
\emph{flavour} of the obscurity barrier (hardcoded keys in mobile
companion apps; undocumented REST surfaces) is what we generalise from.
Industrial hardware would likely have a different flavour (legacy
fieldbus protocols; certificate-pinned but rarely-rotated PKI;
long-lifetime firmware), but the same structural property --- that
obscurity is a labour-cost barrier rather than a cryptographic one ---
would still apply. Validating that conjecture against an industrial
case is left to future work
(\cref{sec:future-work}).

\subsection{Vulnerabilities of IoT-integrator pipelines as a system class}
\label{sec:synthesis-pipeline-vuln}
Beyond the per-device defences, the \emph{IoT-integrator pipeline
itself} --- the AI-assisted workflow that produces the integration ---
has a system-level vulnerability profile that the case studies make
visible. We synthesise the following recurring patterns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Vendor-cloud single point of failure.} Three of the
        four device cases (Ondilo, Balboa, partly EcoFlow) are
        cloud-bound by architecture: a single vendor account, a single
        regional endpoint, a single OAuth2 / Cognito tenant. A
        vendor-side outage, a credential breach, or a unilateral API
        change collapses the integration. The mitigation set
        (refresh-token rotation, secondary onboarding device, sinkhole
        of attribution domains) is \emph{operational} and the
        responsibility of the integrator, not the integration code.
  \item \textbf{Long-lived refresh-token surface.} Ondilo's
        non-expiring refresh tokens and Balboa's 30-day Cognito refresh
        tokens dominate the residual-risk picture in both
        \fp{RESEARCH-PROTOCOL.md} files. A refresh token compromised
        once in plaintext (HA backup, integration log, transcript
        export) outlives almost every other secret in the system.
  \item \textbf{Cross-vendor data-flow opacity.} The Balboa run
        documented BWG\,$\leftrightarrow$\,WaterGuru calls inside the
        Helios corporate group (W-5). Cross-vendor flows are invisible
        at the integration layer and are not exercised by the
        integration's smoke test; their documentation requires either
        DEX deep-dive (T-BAL-9) or a GDPR Subject Access Request
        (T-BAL-12 / T-OND-10).
  \item \textbf{Operational-obscurity anti-pattern.} Sound cryptography
        is not sufficient when the operational layer is misconfigured:
        broken intermediate-CA chains, \texttt{TrustAllStrategy}-equivalent
        symbols on the classpath, and public mobile client secrets
        \emph{re-introduce} an obscurity surface above an
        in-principle-strong authentication scheme. Spider Farmer (no
        auth) and Balboa (sound auth, weak operational layer) bracket
        this pattern.
  \item \textbf{Companion-app permission creep.} Every case had a
        vendor app that exposed FCM, Play Install Referrer, and AD\_ID
        at the manifest layer, and the Balboa app additionally surfaced
        ML Kit Barcode (QR pairing) and Google Mobile Ads SDK strings
        in DEX. The integrator's mitigation (use a secondary onboarding
        device, sinkhole attribution domains) is again operational.
  \item \textbf{Static-only weakness coverage.} Three of the four cases
        performed static-only weakness analysis at the agent layer; the
        reachability of dangerous symbols (e.g.\ Balboa W-3) is held
        back by the absence of a \S{}A-equivalent runtime confirmation.
        The published weakness table is therefore a \emph{lower bound}
        on the device's exposure, not an upper bound.
\end{itemize}

The system-class framing above is consistent with the published large-N
studies of IoT companion apps. Schmidt et al.\ (2023, \emph{ACM CCS})
statically analysed \textbf{9{,}889 companion apps} with the IoTFlow
framework and report exactly the same defect classes --- abandoned
domains, hard-coded credentials, expired certificates, and undisclosed
third-party data sharing [L-IOTAPP-1]. Wang et al.\ (2019, \emph{USENIX
Security}) demonstrated cross-device vulnerability propagation through
\emph{shared} companion-app components on $>$\,4{,}700 devices
[L-IOTAPP-2]. Jin et al.\ (2022, \emph{ACM CCS}, IoTSpotter) found
\textbf{94.11\% of 917 high-install IoT apps with severe cryptographic
violations} at Google-Play scale [L-IOTAPP-3]. OConnor, Jessee \&
Campos~\citep{oconnor2021homealone} (2021, \emph{CSET}) showed that 16
of 20 popular smart-home vendors are vulnerable to companion-app MITM
attacks --- the same defect class as the Balboa W-3 broken-chain +
\texttt{TrustAllStrategy} finding. Mauro Junior et al.\ (2019, \emph{IEEE SPW}) reported that
96 top-selling WiFi IoT devices share only 32 unique companion apps,
and that \textbf{50\% lack proper encryption} [L-IOTAPP-5]. Our four
cases are coherent with this baseline; the contribution of
\cref{sec:synthesis-pipeline-vuln} is the synthesis of the
\emph{integrator-side} mitigations that the prior literature does not
address.

\subsection{Evidence asymmetry between software-side and hardware-side effort-gap compression}
\label{sec:synthesis-evidence-asymmetry}
The \cref{sec:contributions} thesis is supported on the software side by
a peer-reviewed quantitative base (cluster A in
\texttt{docs/sources.md}, entries L-RE-1 through L-RE-8) that includes
a 24.4\% cognitive-burden reduction in AI-assisted decompilation, a
31\%\,$\rightarrow$\,53\% correct-solve rate uplift on
reverse-engineering tasks, and a $>$\,100\% re-executability rate for
AI-assisted code synthesis. The hardware-side extension introduced in
\cref{sec:contributions} rests on a structurally different evidence
base. The single peer-reviewed equipment-cost anchor is the
ChipWhisperer 2014 platform's $\sim$\,100$\times$ reduction
(L-HW-RE-2)\textsuperscript{\ref{fn:hwre-cluster}}. The single
peer-reviewed survey datapoint is Vasile, Oswald \& Chothia
(2018)~\citep{vasile2018breakingallthethings}: an exposed UART
suffices for firmware extraction in more than 45\% of 24 commercial
IoT devices. The skill-floor anchor is Becker et al.\ (2020,
SOUPS)~\citep{becker2020hwreexploratory}: students reach intermediate
proficiency in 14 weeks of structured training. The attack-taxonomy
complement is Papp, Ma \& Butty\'an (2015, IEEE
PST)~\citep{papp2015embedded}, applying a five-dimension taxonomy to a
CVE subset.

Practitioner handbooks bookend the period (Grand 2004 / Huang 2003
$\rightarrow$ van Woudenberg \& O'Flynn 2022,
L-HW-RE-5)\textsuperscript{\ref{fn:hwre-cluster}}; JTAGulator-class
commodity tooling supplements the cluster as grey
literature~\citep{grand2013jtagulator}, and an emerging AI-assisted
PCB-RE literature reports its strongest results at the
component-detection layer rather than at full netlist
reconstruction~\citep{botero2021hwretutorial}.

We make this asymmetry explicit because honest framing of the evidence
base is itself part of the contribution (\texttt{CLAUDE.md} rule 1).
\emph{No peer-reviewed paper publishes a paired ``time-to-extract
firmware on representative device $X$ in 2010 versus 2024''
longitudinal benchmark.} The closest quantitative anchors are the
equipment-cost reduction and the skill-floor pathway; longitudinal
time-to-extract evidence currently exists only as grey literature
(vendor blog posts, conference talks, training-course curricula). We
read this not as a weakness of the hardware-side effort-gap-compression
hypothesis --- the triangulation across cost-floor, skill-floor, survey
datapoint, and bookend handbooks is internally consistent --- but as a
finding about the maturity of empirical hardware-security-research
methodology. Software-side reverse-engineering research has converged
on shared benchmarks, controlled-experiment protocols, and
re-executability metrics; hardware-side reverse-engineering research
has not yet produced a comparable benchmarking infrastructure, and the
absence of paired longitudinal measurements is the most visible
symptom. Closing this gap --- for example by re-running the Vasile et
al.\ 24-device firmware-extraction protocol against contemporary
hardware with contemporary commodity tooling --- would be a productive
direction for future work and would convert the present triangulated
practitioner observation into a benchmarked finding equivalent to the
software-side anchors.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interoperability: the automated right to repair}
\label{sec:disc-r2r}
European decompilation-for-interoperability provisions
(\S{} 69e UrhG, EU 2009/24/EC) presuppose that decompilation is
\emph{legally} possible for an end user. AI assistance makes it
\emph{practically} possible. The case studies are existence proofs that the
legal carve-out can now be exercised at hobbyist scale, not just by
specialised firms. Whether legislatures intended this is an empirical
question for legal scholarship, not for this paper. (Sourcing:
\texttt{docs/sources.md} S-EF-9, S-EF-10, both
\texttt{[unverified-external]} until cross-checked.)

\subsection{The collapse of the boredom barrier}
\label{sec:disc-boredom}
Security through obscurity rested on a labour-market assumption: that a
determined hobbyist would not invest a fortnight to integrate a grow-tent
controller. That assumption is dead. The Spider Farmer case shows three
independent community implementations of the same protocol and an
independently recovered set of MQTT credentials --- converging evidence that
the obscurity barrier has perforated.

\subsection{Asymmetry of collapse}
\label{sec:disc-asymmetry}
The effort gap has \emph{not} collapsed uniformly. AI assistance compresses
the \emph{known-good-protocol} path far more than the
\emph{novel-vulnerability} path; verifying an integration against a live
device is cheap, and verifying an exploit against a live target is not. We
argue this asymmetry is the most under-discussed feature of the post-LLM
threat model.

A live counter-data-point landed during the writing of this paper. On
2026-04-07 Anthropic announced \textbf{Claude Mythos
Preview}~\citep{anthropic2026glasswing,anthropicred2026mythos} ---
a frontier model that, in the company's own published red-team work,
autonomously identified zero-day vulnerabilities in \emph{every major
operating system and every major web browser}.

Specifically: the oldest such finding is a 27-year-old OpenBSD bug;
Mythos chained four-vulnerability browser exploits with JIT
heap-spray, autonomously obtained local privilege escalation via
subtle race conditions and KASLR bypass, and split a multi-gadget ROP
chain across packets to root unauthenticated NFS servers. Crucially
for this paper's thesis, it also reverse-engineers stripped
closed-source binaries to plausible source code at scale --- the
strongest published acknowledgement to date that the offensive-side
gap is closing faster than the L-VD-1\,/\,L-VD-2 era of evidence
(which the \cref{sec:contributions} framing was calibrated against)
suggested.

Mythos is not yet broadly deployed adversarial capability. Anthropic
chose \emph{not} to release it generally, instead launching
\textbf{Project Glasswing}, a coordinated defensive deployment to AWS,
Apple, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorganChase, Linux Foundation,
Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Palo Alto Networks. Anthropic shipped the more
cautious Claude Opus 4.7~\citep{anthropic2026opus47} as the
safeguarded sibling. But the existence proof is now concrete: capable
autonomous offensive AI is on hand at a frontier lab, and the lab
itself describes it as a \emph{``watershed''} demanding
industry-coordinated defence. The asymmetry-of-collapse argument in
this section is not falsified --- verifying an integration is still
cheaper than verifying an exploit --- but the gap is narrower, and
the time horizon over which it remains a defender-favouring
asymmetry is shorter, than this paper's first draft assumed.

\subsection{Dual-use accountability}
\label{sec:disc-dual-use}

\emph{post-hoc} documentation of a collapse that has already happened. In
EcoFlow the surface is documented for the first time here, and we
accordingly enumerate the redactions that must be applied before public
release (logbook 2026-05-01 audit;

outcomes in both directions; the structural answer is a shift in threat

protecting implicitly trusted devices and toward authenticated, scoped
access at every hop.

\paragraph{Guardrails as band-aid: a security-by-design imperative.}
The Project Glasswing model (\cref{sec:disc-asymmetry}) and the
safeguarded Claude Opus 4.7
release~\citep{anthropic2026opus47} are interesting precisely as a
\emph{concrete public example of guardrails as band-aid}. Anthropic's
response to demonstrating Mythos's offensive capability was not to
install guardrails sufficient to release Mythos broadly --- they
explicitly chose not to release it generally, and the safeguards
bolted onto Opus 4.7 are described as ``automatically detect and
block requests that indicate prohibited or high-risk cybersecurity
uses''. This is necessary work, and Anthropic is being unusually
transparent about it. But the band-aid is structural: every guardrail
is a classifier, every classifier has a false-negative rate, and any
false-negative rate combined with autonomous offensive capability and
global proliferation \emph{plausibly} converges to attacker-side
capability over a horizon whose length we cannot yet quantify ---
a claim we present as engineering intuition rather than proof, and
one the
L-MYTHOS evidence base~\citep{anthropic2026glasswing,anthropicred2026mythos}
documents qualitatively but does not yet bound numerically. The author's own observation while writing this
paper (Author's Note: \emph{What surprised me about the assistant})
corroborates this from the inside: Opus 4.7 --- the safeguarded
sibling --- was \emph{cooperatively competent} at the steps an
attacker would also want, and the session was not flagged. The
durable answer is \emph{security by design at the artifact level}
--- published specifications, authenticated and scoped APIs, and
threat models that assume the existence of capable, willing,
AI-augmented adversaries (\cref{fig:dual-use},
\cref{fig:threat-models}). The frame to retire is ``AI is just
another vector''; the frame to adopt is \emph{AI massively increases
the magnitude and frequency of attempts that any given system will
face, and only systems that survive that increase by design will
survive at all}.

\subsection{The paper as evidence for its own thesis}
\label{sec:disc-recursion}
The meta-process case (\cref{sec:case-meta}) is recursive evidence for
our central claim. The same pipeline that compresses the Spider Farmer
and EcoFlow integrations compresses paper-writing --- and exposes the
same dual-use surface. Just as recovered MQTT credentials are a
side-effect of integration work, fabricated citations and unsourced
legal opinions are side-effects of paper work. The discipline that
resolves both is the same: pin artifacts, label provenance, treat AI
output as evidence to be verified rather than as authority.

\subsection{Sloppification: the AI methodological discount}
\label{sec:disc-sloppification}
The most concrete and under-managed risk of AI-assisted research is what
we call the \emph{methodological discount} --- the unspoken trade-off in
which a researcher accepts a degraded standard of evidence in exchange
for AI-assisted speed. Empirical work on LLMs in scientific writing now
documents this risk concretely.
The base-rate evidence is established in
\cref{sec:meta-security}: independent samples by Walters \&
Wilder~\citep{walters2023fabricated},
McGowan~et~al.~\citep{mcgowan2023chatgpt}, and Chelli et
al.~\citep{chelli2024hallucination} bracket the same effect across
multidisciplinary, psychiatric, and systematic-review contexts. Three
further empirical layers extend that base-rate picture:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Buchanan, Hill \& Shapoval~\citep{buchanan2024fabricated},
        \emph{The American Economist}, replicate the fabrication
        finding ($>$30\% non-existent ChatGPT citations) in a third
        discipline, with reliability decreasing as prompts become more
        specific.
  \item Suchak et al.~\citep{suchak2025nhanes}, \emph{PLOS Biology},
        document a real-world consequence: NHANES-based formulaic
        single-factor papers grew from 4 per year (2014--2021) to
        \textbf{190 in the first ten months of 2024} --- direct
        empirical evidence of paper-mill / AI-assisted output flooding
        the literature.
  \item Kendall \& Teixeira da Silva (2024), \emph{Learned Publishing},
        and Sabel \& Larhammar~\citep{sabel2025stockholm}, the
        Stockholm Declaration, frame this at the system level: paper
        mills are now AI-assisted at industrial scale, and the
        publishing model needs structural reform (L-SLOP-5).
\end{itemize}
The mitigation we operationalise is mundane: a verification-status
legend (\texttt{[lit-retrieved]} / \texttt{[lit-read]}) that makes the
read-state of every cited source explicit, plus a refusal to upgrade an
entry without the researcher actually reading the paper.
Sloppification is not solved by a better model; it is solved by labour
discipline that the model cannot perform.

\subsection{Model collapse and the dilution of the scientific commons}
\label{sec:disc-model-collapse}
A second, longer-horizon argument cuts in the opposite direction.
Shumailov et al.\ (2024, \emph{Nature}) showed that \textbf{AI models
trained recursively on AI-generated content
collapse}~\citep{shumailov2024modelcollapse} --- the tails of the
original distribution disappear and successive generations of models
forget the genuine human-data signal. Seddik et
al.~\citep{seddik2024collapse} prove this is unavoidable when training
is purely synthetic but bounded when synthetic and real data are mixed
below a threshold. Gerstgrasser et
al.~\citep{gerstgrasser2024accumulate} extend this further:
\emph{accumulating} real and synthetic data over time (rather than
replacing real with synthetic) can avoid collapse entirely, with a
finite test-error upper bound independent of iteration count.
Suresh et al.\ (2024) characterise the \emph{rate} of collapse for
fundamental distributions (L-MC-5); ForTIFAI / Shabgahi et al.\ (2025)
propose confidence-aware training objectives that delay collapse onset
by $>$2.3$\times$ (L-MC-2).

The connection to our work is direct. Each AI-assisted paper that ships
\emph{un-verified} AI output into the corpus dilutes the ground-truth
signal that future models will be trained on. The two failure modes
--- sloppification at the paper level and model collapse at the corpus
level --- are the same problem at different timescales. They share a
single mitigation: \textbf{preserve the provenance of human-verified,
ground-truth content and keep it cleanly distinguishable from
AI-generated content}. The literature points to a small set of
practices that work against the dilution:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Provenance and read-state labelling at the artifact
        level} --- what \texttt{docs/sources.md} does for citations.
  \item \textbf{Conversation-transcript preservation} --- what
        \texttt{experiments/*/raw\_conversations (copy\&paste, web)/}
        does for AI contributions.
  \item \textbf{Pinning external evidence by commit SHA} --- what the
        \texttt{ffdf60c} audit does for vendor code.
  \item \textbf{Mixing rather than replacing}: keep human-authored data
        alongside AI-assisted output rather than letting the AI output
        substitute for it (L-MC-3, L-MC-4).
  \item \textbf{Disclosure and detection at the publication level} ---
        guidelines from Cheng et al.\ (2025) and Pellegrina et al.\
        (2025); Stockholm Declaration governance proposals from Sabel
        et al.\ (2025) (L-SLOP-7, L-SLOP-10, L-SLOP-12).
  \item \textbf{Filtering high-confidence-token artifacts} during model
        training --- ForTIFAI (L-MC-2); not actionable for paper
        authors but actionable for foundation-model trainers.
\end{itemize}
We do not claim to solve model collapse. We claim that the methodology
in \cref{sec:methodology} is consistent with what the literature
suggests works against it, and that publishing AI-assisted research
without provenance and disclosure is, at the corpus level, an
externality on every future model.

\subsection{Methodological implications for security research}
\label{sec:disc-methodology}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Treat AI conversation transcripts as research artifacts. They are
        the equivalent of a lab notebook for the LLM era.
  \item Pin all external code by commit SHA, not by branch name. Branches
        move; SHAs do not.
  \item Embed vendor artifacts under explicit redistribution caveats rather
        than referencing moving URLs.
  \item Mark every literature claim with a verification status. The
        discipline is cheap; the alternative is the current state of
        practice (\cref{sec:disc-sloppification}).
  \item Disclose AI usage explicitly. The disclosure is not an apology
        --- it is the unit of accountability that lets the work be
        audited rather than guessed at.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Threats to validity}
\label{sec:disc-threats}
\label{sec:disc-validity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Selection bias.} Cases were chosen because the researcher
        already wanted local control; failed attempts at harder targets are
        under-reported.
  \item \textbf{Tooling drift.} AI model behaviour changes between
        sessions. The workflow is reproducible against artifacts; identical
        AI outputs are not.
  \item \textbf{Legal framing.} AI-generated legal analysis in transcripts
        is not legal advice and is flagged as such in the source register.
  \item \textbf{Redistribution.} Vendor APKs and PDFs are vendored for
        cite-ability but their redistribution status is not yet resolved.
  \item \textbf{Sloppification risk in this paper.} The literature
        register (\cref{sec:meta-security},
        \cref{sec:disc-sloppification}) is currently
        \texttt{[lit-retrieved]} only. No claim in this paper depends
        on a literature citation that has not been read in full by the
        researcher; we have explicitly preferred to leave a claim
        unsupported rather than cite a paper we have not read.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proliferation of hacking: societal and systemic risk}
\label{sec:disc-proliferation}
The collapse of the effort gap is not only a bilateral event between a
single researcher and a single vendor. It has a \emph{proliferation}
dimension: when a capability that previously required specialist knowledge
becomes accessible to anyone with a chat interface, the distribution of
actors who can exercise that capability widens dramatically.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Volume risk.} A barrier that previously filtered out all
        but the most motivated attackers now filters out only those who
        choose not to engage. The population of capable attackers grows
        without any corresponding shrinkage in the installed base of
        vulnerable devices.
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric uplift.} The interoperability researcher and
        the adversary operate against the same technical surface, but the
        adversary has no obligation to stop at a working integration.
        The AI workflow yields the protocol map and the credential surface
        as a byproduct of integration work.
  \item \textbf{Normalisation effect.} As AI-assisted RE becomes common,
        the cultural friction that previously discouraged casual
        exploitation disappears. This normalisation is distinct from the
        technical capability gain and may be harder to reverse.
  \item \textbf{Tooling acceleration.} Community RE workflows are
        increasingly trained on by successive model generations. The
        marginal cost of the next RE task is lower than the last --- a
        compounding effect rather than a one-time step change.
\end{itemize}

The response is not to suppress the methodology but to argue explicitly
for the countervailing obligations: open APIs, published specifications,
credential rotation, and zero-trust architecture at the device level.
Obscurity that has already been defeated offers no protection; the goal
must shift to making the attack surface unattractive rather than
invisible.

\subsection{Prompt injection in obfuscated software as a countermeasure}
\label{sec:disc-prompt-injection}
A speculative but technically coherent countermeasure deserves mention:
\emph{adversarial prompt injection embedded in the vendor artifact itself}.
If the primary attack surface is an LLM analysing vendor code or APK
strings, a vendor could in principle embed strings designed to manipulate
the AI's analysis --- plausible-but-invalid cryptographic constants that
an LLM might surface as ground truth, false protocol documentation, or
safety-triggering content designed to invoke the model's refusal
behaviours.

The feasibility and ethics of this approach are genuinely contested:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Feasibility.} Modern LLMs are increasingly resistant to
        naive injection attempts. A determined attacker with a custom
        agent would likely filter vendor-embedded strings; the defence
        degrades faster than the attack capability it counteracts.
  \item \textbf{Ethics and legality.} A vendor deploying adversarial
        injection strings in a consumer product raises
        \S{} 69e UrhG / EU 2009/24/EC interoperability questions: does
        the injection constitute an additional technical protection measure
        (\emph{TPM}) under EU Directive 2001/29/EC? Does it breach
        good-faith obligations toward legitimate interoperability
        researchers?
  \item \textbf{Systemic cost.} Injection artefacts in vendor code would
        propagate into training corpora and degrade general-purpose
        code-analysis models for legitimate users --- a negative
        externality of the same type as synthetic sloppification
        (\cref{sec:disc-sloppification}, \cref{sec:disc-model-collapse}).
\end{itemize}

The most robust conclusion is that prompt injection is not a viable
primary defence. It can impose a modest cost on na\"ive attacks but
provides no durable protection against a determined adversary. The
structural answer remains: design systems that do not depend on secret
protocols.

\subsection{Privacy as a user right: keeping the device, dropping the cloud}
\label{sec:disc-privacy}
The interoperability framing in
\cref{sec:disc-r2r} is incomplete without its sibling: the
right to \emph{use the device as intended} without exporting telemetry to
the vendor cloud. This is a separate user right, anchored in EU GDPR
Art.\ 5(1)(c) (data minimisation), Art.\ 25 (data protection by design and
by default), Art.\ 21 (right to object), and --- in German constitutional
doctrine --- \emph{informationelle Selbstbestimmung} (informational
self-determination, \emph{BVerfG, Volksz\"ahlungsurteil} 1983). The
empirical literature documents that the consumer-IoT baseline routinely
violates the spirit of these provisions, and that AI-assisted local
integrations are one of the few user-side instruments that materially
close the gap.

\paragraph{The empirical baseline is severe.}
Ren et al.~\citep{ren2019iotinforming} is the cornerstone measurement:
81 consumer IoT devices in US/UK labs, 34{,}586 controlled experiments
characterising destinations, encryption state, inferable interactions,
and unexpected exposures (including a recording device that covertly
transmitted video). Apthorpe et al.\ (2017) showed that even when the
payload \emph{is} encrypted, traffic-rate side channels reveal
sensitive user interactions to a passive observer such as the user's
ISP for four representative devices (sleep monitor, indoor camera,
smart switch, smart speaker) [L-PRIV-2]. Acar et
al.~\citep{acar2020wisec} (WiSec 2020) reported $>$\,90\% accuracy in
identifying device states and ongoing user activities from
passively-sniffed encrypted traffic across WiFi, ZigBee, and BLE. Apthorpe et al.\ (2018) surveyed 1{,}731 US adults across
3{,}840 contextual-integrity information flows and found that the egress
patterns documented in the measurement studies are \emph{contrary to}
what users hold as smart-home privacy norms, not just contrary to a
regulatory ideal [L-PRIV-4].

\paragraph{The companion-app surface compounds the cloud surface.}
Most consumer IoT devices route a second telemetry path through their
mobile companion app. Nan et al.~\citep{nan2023iotapp} statically
analysed \textbf{6{,}208} IoT companion apps and identified
\textbf{1{,}973} that exposed user data --- health status, home
address, behavioural patterns --- to first or third parties without
proper disclosure, covering devices from at least \textbf{1{,}559
distinct vendors}.
Tazi et al.\ (2025) and Neupane et al.\ (2022) audited 455 IoT companion
apps and found systematic over-requesting of permissions unrelated to
the apps' stated purpose, with two of the analysed apps transmitting
credentials in unencrypted form [L-PRIV-6, L-PRIV-8]. Chatzoglou et al.\
(2022) confirmed the same pattern across $>$\,40 chart-topping Android
IoT apps from six device categories [L-PRIV-7].

\paragraph{Local interoperability is a privacy mitigation that
preserves intended use.}
The Spider Farmer (\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer}) and EcoFlow PowerOcean
(\cref{sec:case-ecoflow}) integrations are concrete examples: replacing
the vendor-cloud path with a Home Assistant custom integration changes
the data flow from ``device $\to$ vendor cloud $\to$ third parties + ISP
observer'' to ``device $\to$ user's LAN $\to$ user's local store''. The
device continues to perform its advertised function --- switching the
grow-tent fan, reporting the battery state of charge --- but the
telemetry that previously left the home does not. Kazlouski et al.\
(2022) provide the closest published existence proof for the general
principle: Fitbit trackers correctly report steps, workouts, and sleep
duration/quality (and synchronise with six partner apps) when
third-party domains are blocked at the network layer; each studied app
contacted between 1 and 20 \emph{non-required} third parties [L-PRIV-9].
The user-side instrument and the vendor-side default differ by exactly
that delta. Kounoudes et al.\ (2020) systematise the mapping from such
user-side practices onto GDPR Art.\ 5 / Art.\ 25 [L-PRIV-10].

\paragraph{Counter-evidence: regulation alone is not the answer.}
Kollnig et al.\ (2021) studied tracking in nearly two million Android
apps before and after GDPR and found \emph{limited change} in the
prevalence of third-party tracking, with concentration among large
gatekeepers persisting [L-PRIV-11]. George et al.\ (2019) further
document ``transient processing'' patterns that escape the regulation's
scope altogether [L-PRIV-12]. The lesson is not that GDPR has failed
but that statutory rights to data minimisation require user-side
technical alternatives to be exercised, not just declared. AI-assisted
local integration is one such alternative --- and, as
\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer,sec:case-ecoflow} show, it is now within
reach of an individual hobbyist on a Saturday afternoon, not only of
specialised firms.

\paragraph{Implication for the paper's central thesis.}
The collapse of the effort gap is double-edged in security terms
(\cref{sec:disc-dual-use}) but it is \emph{single-edged and net-positive}
in privacy terms: the same workflow that exposes a vendor's MQTT broker
also lets the device's owner keep using the device while opting out of
the vendor's data pipeline. Privacy as a user right thus joins
right-to-repair (\cref{sec:disc-r2r}) as a substantive
rather than incidental beneficiary of the methodology. A vendor
objection of the form ``users cannot exercise their privacy rights
without breaking the product'' is, after these case studies,
empirically disprovable: the product still works.

\subsection{The malicious IoT-integrator agent}
\label{sec:disc-malicious-integrator}
The IoT-Integrator prompt
(\texttt{docs/prompts/iot-integrator-prompt.md}) is, by design,
dual-use. The same self-augmenting, four-phase protocol that produced
the Ondilo and Balboa case studies under researcher governance can be
re-instantiated, with no change to the prompt body, by an adversary
whose intent is exploitation rather than interoperability. The Phase-2
weakness analysis is the inflection point: a researcher version stops
at an annotated weakness table and a configuration-only Phase~3
deliverable; an adversarial version proceeds directly to a working
exploit pipeline, suppresses the dual-use-mirror discipline, and skips
the redaction policy.

The differential is not technical capability --- both versions consume
the same APK, the same DEX strings, the same Cognito discovery
endpoint --- but governance: which checkpoints fire, which transcripts
are preserved, which weaknesses are upstreamed, and which controls are
recommended to the operator. The threat surface of a malicious
integrator agent is enumerated below so that the methodology's openness
is paid for honestly:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Per-device exploit pipeline.} A malicious version would
        convert each Weakness Table row directly into a runnable
        exploit script rather than into a hardening control. The W-3
        reachability question (Balboa \texttt{TrustAllStrategy})
        becomes ``land a man-in-the-middle and capture refresh tokens
        at scale'' rather than ``file a \S{}A jadx run''.
  \item \textbf{Credential-and-token harvesting at integration time.}
        The integration smoke tests consume valid OAuth2 / Cognito
        tokens; an adversarial integrator agent embedded in a trojaned
        community integration could exfiltrate them. The Spider Farmer
        case is a precedent at a smaller scale: a community thread
        already published the recovered MQTT broker credentials. An
        LLM-driven integrator could automate that disclosure path
        across thousands of devices.
  \item \textbf{Suppression of the dual-use-mirror.} The
        \texttt{T-DUAL-USE-MIRROR} technique is the discipline that
        pairs every weakness with its attacker counterpart in writing.
        A malicious operator simply removes the mirror column and ships
        the weakness table as a target list.
  \item \textbf{Self-augmentation of attack capability.} The same
        self-augmenting loop that lets each new researcher run inherit
        prior techniques (\texttt{T-CROSS-VENDOR-CORPORATE-FLOW},
        \texttt{T-OPERATIONAL-OBSCURITY}) is open to an adversarial
        corpus: each new exploit run extends the attacker's technique
        inventory. The doubling time of the inventory is bounded only
        by the number of devices the adversary chooses to enumerate.
  \item \textbf{Trust laundering through a benign-looking artifact.}
        A malicious integrator can publish a Home Assistant custom
        integration that \emph{also} exfiltrates household
        credentials. The community-trust signal that legitimises an
        upstream community integration such as
        \texttt{[REDACTED:repo-path:BALBOA-UPSTREAM-1]} or
        \texttt{[REDACTED:repo-path:BALBOA-UPSTREAM-2]} --- a working
        integration plus a non-empty README --- is exactly what an
        adversary needs to forge.
  \item \textbf{Erosion of the governance baseline.} As
        malicious-integrator output enters public corpora (forks,
        gists, model training sets), future researcher-side IoT
        Integrator runs may inherit adversarial techniques
        \emph{unknowingly} through the same self-augmenting mechanism
        that is the methodology's strength. This is the
        \cref{sec:disc-model-collapse} model-collapse problem applied
        to an agent's technique inventory rather than to a paper's
        literature register.
\end{itemize}

The empirical literature on adversarial LLM agents corroborates each of
the failure modes above. Fang et al.\ (2024) showed that \textbf{GPT-4
autonomously exploits 87\% of 15 one-day CVEs given only the CVE
description} (versus 0\% for GPT-3.5 and open-source LLMs), and
autonomously hacks websites with no prior knowledge of the vulnerability
[L-AGT-1, L-AGT-2]. Lupinacci et al.\ (2025) evaluated 18
state-of-the-art LLMs and found \textbf{94.4\% succumb to direct prompt
injection, 83.3\% to RAG backdoor attacks, and 100\% to inter-agent
trust exploitation} [L-AGT-4] --- a direct empirical anchor for the
trust-laundering bullet. Chen et al.\ (2024) demonstrated AgentPoison,
in which backdoor triggers achieve $>$\,80\% attack success at
$<$\,0.1\% poison rate of an agent's memory or RAG knowledge base, with
no fine-tuning required [L-AGT-6]; Yang et al.\ (2024) showed that
backdoors can manipulate intermediate reasoning steps while preserving
the final-output check that a reviewer would inspect [L-AGT-7]; Wang
et al.\ (2024, BadAgent) showed that backdoors are robust \emph{even
after fine-tuning on trustworthy data}, refuting the assumption that
capability-side defences suffice [L-AGT-10]. Lee et al.\ (2025) bypassed
refusal-trained safeguards in Claude for Computer Use with a
24.41\%--41.33\% attack-success rate [L-AGT-5], and Ferrag et al.\
(2025) systematise more than thirty attack techniques in LLM-agent
ecosystems including the ``Toxic Agent Flow'' exploit pattern that maps
directly onto the Phase-2 inflection point [L-AGT-3]. Zhang et al.\
(2024, ASB) report an \textbf{84.30\% average attack success rate}
across a 27-method $\times$ 13-backbone $\times$ 10-scenario benchmark
[L-AGT-8].

The mitigation set is structural rather than technical and consistent
with the rest of the paper. Pin the governance prompt by commit SHA at
every run; require explicit researcher checkpoints between phases.
Preserve the dual-use mirror as an immutable column; surface every
weakness \emph{with} its mitigation in the same artifact; and treat
the technique inventory as a redaction-aware document rather than a
raw attack-capability log. None of these mitigations defeat a
determined adversary; they raise the cost of laundering an exploit
pipeline as a research artifact, which is the cost we can credibly
raise. The literature is consistent with this framing. Capability-side
defences (safety training, fine-tuning on trusted data) are
demonstrably insufficient against adversarial agents [L-AGT-10,
L-AGT-7]; governance-side defences (provenance, checkpoints,
redaction discipline) are the only durable answer the field currently
offers.

\subsection{Automated mass probing of public APK repositories}
\label{sec:disc-apk-mass-probing}
A second adversarial scenario follows directly from the cost structure
documented in this paper: if a single APK can be statically analysed by
an AI-assisted pipeline in under an hour and yield a usable weakness
table, then \emph{the entire enumerable surface of public APK
repositories} is in scope for a single adversary. Public APK mirrors
--- APKPure, APKMirror, APKCombo, F-Droid, and the Google Play crawl
indices --- collectively distribute on the order of millions of APKs.
The Balboa case study sourced its artifact from APKPure
(\texttt{com.controlmyspa.ownerappnew}) and the Ondilo case study used
the APKPure listing for \texttt{fr.ondilo.ico.icomanager} as its
manifest source; both flows generalise.

The published literature already documents the per-vendor base rate.
Sivakumaran et al.~\citep{sivakumaran2023bleguuide} (2023) found
\textbf{$>$70\% of 17{,}243 BLE-enabled Android APKs contain at least
one security vulnerability}; Nan et al.~\citep{nan2023iotapp} (2023)
statically analysed \textbf{6{,}208 IoT companion apps} and identified
\textbf{1{,}973} that exposed user data, covering devices from at
least \textbf{1{,}559 distinct vendors}. Corpus-scale precedents
extend across the wider Android ecosystem: Chen et al.\ (2015, MassVet)
vetted \textbf{1.2 million apps from 33 markets} at 10-second-per-app
throughput and captured $>$\,100{,}000 malicious apps including 20+
likely zero-day [L-APK-3]; Ishii et al.\ (2017, APPraiser) analysed
\textbf{1.3 million apps} and found that 76\% of clones in third-party
marketplaces are malware [L-APK-4]; Hou et al.\ (2022, ANDSCANNER)
measured 6{,}261 firmware images from 153 vendors and 38 newfound
CVEs/CNVDs [L-APK-5]; Gao et al.\ (2021) reconstructed 28{,}564
versioned app lineages across 5 million packages [L-APK-6]; Sanna
et al.\ (2024) extended the same shape to native code across
$>$\,100{,}000 apps [L-APK-7]. Earlier marketplace-side work --- Zhou
et al.\ (2012, DroidMOSS, 5--13\% of apps in third-party marketplaces
are repackaged) [L-APK-1] and Vidas \& Christin (2013, 41{,}057 apps
from 194 alternative Android markets, some markets distributing almost
exclusively malware) [L-APK-2] --- establishes that the operator-side
gatekeeping problem predates the AI-assisted regime. All of these
studies were \emph{human-led} static analyses with substantial
team-years of effort. AI assistance compresses the per-APK cost into a
regime where the limit is no longer analyst time but storage,
bandwidth, and rate-limit politeness toward the host repository. The new threat surface is
enumerated below:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sweep at corpus scale.} A single LLM-driven crawler
        can enumerate the IoT-companion-app subset of APKPure
        ($\sim$tens of thousands of apps) and run the same
        \texttt{T-APK-STRINGS} / \texttt{T-MANIFEST-PERMISSION-AUDIT} /
        \texttt{T-REST-WRITE-PROBE} pipeline on each. The output is a
        vendor-by-vendor weakness inventory comparable in coverage to
        Sivakumaran et al.\ but produced in days rather than years.
  \item \textbf{Identity-provider enumeration.} A trivial extension of
        the Balboa Phase-2 finding
        (\texttt{iot.controlmyspa.com/idm/tokenEndpoint} discovery
        returns a Cognito user-pool client + secret) is a corpus-wide
        enumeration of vendor identity-provider configurations,
        including which providers ship a public mobile client secret,
        which expose \texttt{AdminUserGlobalSignOut}-equivalent
        administrative endpoints, and which permit unauthenticated
        discovery.
  \item \textbf{Cross-vendor data-flow harvest.} The W-5 cross-vendor
        pattern (BWG\,$\leftrightarrow$\,WaterGuru inside the Helios
        corporate group) is detectable from APK strings alone. A
        corpus-wide harvest produces a corporate-group ownership graph
        plus the inter-company data-flow edges, useful both to a
        privacy researcher and to an attacker building a
        targeted-campaign list.
  \item \textbf{Trojaned-APK detection at scale, and its inverse.} The
        same pipeline that detects malicious analytics SDKs at scale
        (a defensive use) detects naïve credential storage, hardcoded
        keys, and plaintext API keys at scale (an offensive use). The
        asymmetry argument of \cref{sec:disc-asymmetry} applies: the
        AI-assisted defender and the AI-assisted attacker pay the same
        per-APK cost, but the attacker has no obligation to coordinate
        disclosure.
  \item \textbf{APK-repository operator as gatekeeper.} Public APK
        mirrors operate without strong rate-limiting or robust
        authenticated-fetch requirements. They are now \emph{de facto}
        a critical infrastructure for both interoperability research
        and adversarial reconnaissance. A repository-side mitigation
        (rate-limit, abuse-detection, vendor-of-record disclosure) is
        the leverage point but is largely absent today.
  \item \textbf{Legal and ethical posture.} Mass corpus probing is
        \emph{not} covered by the \S{}\,69e UrhG / EU 2009/24/EC
        interoperability carve-out applied per device in
        \cref{sec:disc-r2r}; the carve-out presumes a specific
        interoperability goal between a specific user and a specific
        product. Corpus-wide enumeration is closer to security
        research, which has its own legal regime (computer-misuse
        statutes, vendor terms of service, DMCA \S{}\,1201(f) in the
        US). The legal posture is unsettled.
\end{itemize}

The honest assessment is that this scenario is \emph{already feasible}
given the per-case cost documented in
\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer,sec:case-ecoflow,sec:synthesis-iot-integrator}:
the marginal cost of the next APK is a few cents of inference time.
Whether a corpus-scale probe is run by a defender (with coordinated
disclosure) or by an adversary (silently, then sold) is a governance
question, not a capability question. The mitigation set is again
structural: repository-side abuse-detection, vendor-side
identity-provider hardening (closing W-3-class operational-obscurity
weaknesses), and a community norm that publication of corpus-scale
findings carries a coordinated-disclosure obligation comparable to
per-vendor vulnerability research today.

\subsection{Scope and limitations of the study}
\label{sec:disc-scope-limits}
The scope of the empirical contribution is bounded along five
dimensions. The limitations distributed across
\cref{sec:scope-non-goals,sec:synthesis-limits,sec:disc-validity}
and the new \cref{sec:synthesis-pipeline-vuln,sec:disc-malicious-integrator,sec:disc-apk-mass-probing}
are consolidated here so that a reader can read the perimeter of the
work without reconstructing it from disparate paragraphs.

\paragraph{1. Case-base size, selection, and segment.}
The empirical base is four device-integration case studies plus one
recursive meta-process case
(\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer,sec:case-ecoflow,sec:case-meta,sec:synthesis-iot-integrator}).
All four device cases are \emph{consumer-market} IoT products in the
residential / horticulture / pool-and-spa / energy segment, sampled
through researcher need rather than randomly. Industrial / OT / ICS /
safety-critical hardware is \emph{not} covered (cf.\
\cref{sec:synthesis-limits}); the inference we draw is narrower ---
that the \emph{flavour} of the obscurity barrier (hardcoded keys;
undocumented REST surfaces; operational-obscurity at the TLS layer) is
generalisable, not that the per-device defences are equivalent across
segments. Validation against an industrial case is left to future work
(\cref{sec:future-work}).

\paragraph{2. AI-assistance is the unit of analysis, not LLM capability per se.}
The contribution is the AI-assisted \emph{workflow} (artifacts,
transcripts, provenance, governance) rather than any single model's
capability. Tooling drift (\cref{sec:disc-threats}) means identical
prompts will not produce identical outputs at later dates; the
workflow and committed artifacts are the reproducible unit. The
L-AGT-* literature shows that frontier-LLM offensive capability has
already been demonstrated at one-day-CVE scale [L-AGT-1, L-AGT-2];
this paper does not measure that capability, it measures what a
structured \emph{governance} layer adds on top of it.

\paragraph{3. Validation completeness varies across cases.}
Spider Farmer and EcoFlow validation was completed against vendor code
at commit \texttt{ffdf60c}; the Ondilo and Balboa runs are
\emph{cross-validation} of the methodology with researcher-side device
validation still pending (test checklists \texttt{T-OND-1..T-OND-10}
and \texttt{T-BAL-1..T-BAL-12} in the per-case
\fp{RESEARCH-PROTOCOL.md}). Until those checklists are executed,
the two new cases enter the paper as evidence for the methodology's
\emph{transferability}, not for end-to-end integration correctness on
the specific physical devices.

\paragraph{4. Literature and legal framing are \texttt{[lit-retrieved]}, not \texttt{[lit-read]}.}
Every literature citation in this paper is anchored through the
verification-status legend in \texttt{docs/sources.md}; clusters A--O
are currently \texttt{[lit-retrieved]}-only. The paper's claims are
explicitly held back from depending on any single citation that has
not been read in full. German / EU primary legal sources (\S{}\,69e
UrhG, EU 2009/24/EC) and vendor-published positions on community RE
remain \texttt{[needs-research]} (cf.\ \cref{sec:scope} and
the \cref{sec:ai-disclosure-disclaimers} disclaimer that AI-generated legal
analysis is not legal advice). The
\cref{sec:disc-apk-mass-probing} ``legal posture is unsettled''
framing for mass corpus probing is a deliberate honesty move, not a
substantive legal claim.

\paragraph{5. The study's outputs are themselves dual-use.}
The same artifacts that operationalise transparency --- pinned APK
hashes, weakness tables with technique tags, runnable smoke tests,
executable agent prompts --- are the inputs an adversarial integrator
needs (\cref{sec:disc-malicious-integrator}). Likewise, the
corpus-scale extension (\cref{sec:disc-apk-mass-probing}) is feasible
\emph{because} the per-APK cost has collapsed. We accept this
asymmetry as the price of methodological openness; the structural
mitigations (rule-12 redaction policy, dual-use mirror per Weakness
Table row, configuration-only Phase 3 outcomes, no public push or
Zenodo deposit without explicit researcher consent per rule 13) raise
the cost of laundering the methodology as an exploit pipeline but do
not eliminate it. The paper does not claim to have solved this
problem; it claims to have \emph{made it visible} in a form that can
be reasoned about and audited.

Two further constraints are worth naming explicitly because they are
not classical ``threats to validity'' but bound the \emph{interpretation}
of the work. First, the recursive meta-process case
(\cref{sec:case-meta}) is included as evidence for the methodology,
\emph{not} as independent confirmation of the central thesis
(\cref{sec:synthesis-limits}); a reader is entitled to discount it
accordingly. Second, the four-case difficulty taxonomy in
\cref{sec:synthesis-difficulty} is \emph{qualitative} and assigned by
the researcher from the per-case \texttt{REPORT.md} evidence; the
composite rating is informative of the \emph{spread} across cases,
not of an absolute scale, and a reader should not treat ``Easy'' /
``Medium'' as calibrated against any external benchmark.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

AI-assisted reverse engineering does not invent new capabilities; it lowers
the activation energy of capabilities that already existed. The Spider
Farmer and EcoFlow PowerOcean cases show that this lowering is enough to
collapse the effort gap that previously sustained ``security through
obscurity'' as a viable consumer-IoT defence. The collapse is genuinely
double-edged --- it materially advances right-to-repair while exposing live
attack surfaces --- but it is also \emph{asymmetric}, compressing the
integration path more aggressively than the offensive path.

The path forward is not to discourage AI-assisted research but to make it
\emph{auditable}: vendor artifacts pinned, transcripts treated as evidence,
dual-use evaluation built into the methodology rather than appended to it,
and legal framing always sourced. Obscurity is dead. What replaces it has
to be designed, not assumed.

\subsection{A terminological precision before the call-to-action}
\label{sec:ai-vs-genai}

A persistent source of confusion in the public debate --- and,
increasingly, in the academic one --- is the conflation of
\emph{artificial intelligence} in the broad methodological sense (the
family of methods spanning symbolic systems, classical machine learning,
deep learning, reinforcement learning, and the present generation of
large language models) with \emph{generative AI} in the narrow sense
(the LLM-and-diffusion-model subset that drives the current hype cycle).
The two are not interchangeable. The empirical effort-gap collapse
documented in~\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer,sec:case-ecoflow}, and the
meta-process collapse documented in~\cref{sec:case-meta}, are driven
specifically by the \emph{generative-AI} subset; the broader AI methods
family --- adversarial-machine-learning detectors for hardcoded secrets,
classical static analysers, symbolic execution engines, supervised
classifiers for vulnerability triage --- has been shaping the
security-research workflow for years and will continue to do so after
the present hype cycle subsides. Throughout this paper, where the
distinction matters, \emph{generative AI} (or \emph{Gen AI} / LLMs)
refers to the narrow subset and \emph{AI} refers to the broader methods
family. The call-to-action that follows is framed against the broader
family on purpose: norms calibrated only to the current LLM cohort will
age out of relevance the moment the cohort does, whereas norms
calibrated to the \emph{AI methods family as a whole} are the ones we
will still need in 2030.

\subsection{Call-to-action: generate the norms while the practice is young}
\label{sec:call-to-action}

The empirical moment this paper documents is unusual: we are among the
first cohort of researchers to experience AI-assisted scientific work as
a routine modality rather than an experimental novelty. That position
carries a corresponding obligation --- to use the window in which the
practice is still being formed to \emph{generate the norms of acceptable
use}, rather than to inherit them, retrofit them, or have them imposed
after the fact. Even on the conservative reading of the present hype
cycle --- that current Gen AI capabilities will plateau, that funding
will contract, and that a portion of the integration tooling will recede
--- the underlying AI methods family will not leave the scientific
workflow. From the present moment onwards, AI-assisted research is part
of how science is produced; the open question is the \emph{discipline}
under which it is produced.

We argue that the eight integrated practices developed in~\cref{sec:pandora}
--- transcript preservation, verification-status labelling, provenance
maps, mirror discipline, the recursive meta-process case study,
base-rate-anchored AI disclosure, legal honesty about authorship, and
FAIR alignment as a precondition --- are best understood as a
\emph{condition catalogue} for AI-assisted work to be defensible as
research. They are not a manifesto; they are a checklist whose items
can be argued with row by row. The call-to-action is correspondingly
modest: that the community treat methodological norm-setting for
AI-assisted research as itself a research activity, conducted now, in
the open, and with the same evidentiary discipline we expect of
empirical claims.

\subsection{Integrating the AI-skeptic position: from opponents to co-norm-setters}
\label{sec:ai-skeptic-integration}

A second strand of the contemporary debate must be addressed directly
rather than dismissed. A widely held position --- sometimes voiced as
outright opposition to AI in research, sometimes as a quieter accusation
that AI-assisted authorship is a form of cheating --- points at real
harms: undisclosed authorship that crosses into plagiarism
(\cref{sec:disc-sloppification}), the documented degradation of the
scientific commons by paper-mill output~\citep{suchak2025nhanes}, and
the collapsing baseline of trust in academic text when readers cannot
tell what a human wrote and what a model produced. We take that
position to be \emph{legitimate}, and we read its underlying claim ---
that the current default of undisclosed AI assistance is corrosive ---
as substantially correct.

The eight practices are our proposal for the conditions under which the
legitimate concern is \emph{answerable} rather than dismissible.
Transcript-as-artifact (practice~1) makes the AI's contribution
inspectable rather than hidden; the verification-status ladder
(practice~2) makes the read-state of every cited source explicit rather
than indeterminate; explicit, base-rate-anchored disclosure
(practice~6) replaces the ``AI was used to assist'' boilerplate with an
audit trail. Read in this register, the eight practices are an offer:
to skeptics for whom undisclosed AI use is the principal harm, here is a
discipline under which AI-assisted work can be evaluated on its own
terms. The intended move is from opposition to \emph{co-norm-setting}
--- disagreement about which practices belong on the list, which ones
are insufficient, and which ones the next generation of tooling will
subsume is precisely the kind of engagement we are most hoping to
provoke. The Author's Note above
(\emph{Open to constructive criticism --- actively}) is the same
invitation in personal-voice form.

\subsection{A candidate FAIR for AI-Assisted Research extension}
\label{sec:fair4ai}

The community already has two precedents for adapting the FAIR Guiding
Principles to non-data artefact classes. \textbf{FAIR4RS} --- the FAIR
Principles for Research Software, released in 2022 by the FAIR for
Research Software Working Group jointly convened by the Research
Software Alliance, FORCE11, and the Research Data Alliance --- extends
FAIR to source code, scripts, computational workflows, and executables
created during the research process~\citep{chuehong2022fair4rs}.
\textbf{FAIR4ML} --- the metadata schema developed by the RDA FAIR for
Machine Learning Interest Group (schema v0.1.0, 2024) --- extends FAIR
to machine-learning \emph{models} and \emph{model evaluations}, building
on Schema.org and CodeMeta and now adopted in production by services
including InesData and MLentory~\citep{rda2024fair4ml}. Neither of
these directly addresses what AI-mediated \emph{research processes}
produce: exportable conversation transcripts, versioned prompts,
tool-and-model version manifests, verification-status ladders,
structured redaction policies for credentials and personal data.

We propose, as a target for community refinement rather than as a
finished standard, that the eight integrated practices in~\cref{sec:pandora}
be developed into a \textbf{FAIR for AI-Assisted Research} extension
(working name: \emph{FAIR4AI}). A first-cut mapping is:
\textbf{Findability} --- persistent, citable identifiers for AI
conversation sessions, alongside the existing data and software
identifiers (DOIs, Software Heritage IDs); \textbf{Accessibility} --- a
standardised redaction policy under which credentials, personal data,
device serial numbers, and other rule-12 categories can be removed from
public-facing artefacts without breaking the audit trail (cf.\
\texttt{docs/redaction-policy.md}); \textbf{Interoperability} --- a
transcript-export format, prompt-version manifest, and
verification-status legend that travel with the manuscript across
publishing platforms rather than being trapped in a single vendor's chat
UI; \textbf{Reusability} --- exportable transcripts plus versioned
prompts plus declared tool and model versions, sufficient for an
independent reader to reconstruct \emph{which decision was made under
which AI assistance under which discipline}. The repository
accompanying this paper already practises de-facto versions of every
element on that list (\texttt{CLAUDE.md} rules~4 and~14; the
verification-status ladder in \texttt{docs/sources.md}; the redaction
policy in \texttt{docs/redaction-policy.md}). Naming what we are
already doing, and inviting the community to refine, replace, or reject
the candidate, is itself the call-to-action: norms generated by the
practitioners who use the tools, recorded openly, and surrendered to
peer scrutiny before they harden into convention. As of 2026-05-04 we
have not located an existing community working group convened under
this name (\texttt{docs/sources.md} L-FAIR-3, status
\texttt{[needs-research]}); if one is formed, or already exists under
an adjacent name, the proposal here defers to it.

\emph{(i) Authorship and the copyrightability of AI outputs.}
\S{}~2 UrhG requires a \emph{pers\"onliche geistige Sch\"opfung}. The
prevailing reading (consistent with the long \emph{Sch\"opfungsh\"ohe}
tradition and with EU CJEU case law on the \emph{Werkbegriff}) is that
purely AI-generated text or images do not, on their own, satisfy this
requirement and therefore do not enjoy independent copyright protection
in Germany. Human curation, selection, prompting, and editing can create
a copyrightable human contribution on top of the AI output, but the AI
itself is not an author. (Sourcing: \texttt{docs/sources.md} S-EF-9 ---
primary text of UrhG --- remains \texttt{[unverified-external]}; this
footnote is an informal restatement of the prevailing reading and must
be replaced with sourced legal commentary before any legal claim is
made.) \emph{(ii) Text-and-data-mining and AI training.}
\S{}~44b UrhG (introduced in 2021 to implement Art.~4 of the EU DSM
Directive 2019/790) creates a TDM exception for both research and
commercial uses, with a machine-readable opt-out for commercial use.
The first major German judgment on AI training and copyright ---
\emph{Kneschke v LAION} before LG M\"unchen~I (Az.\ 42~O~14139/23,
October 2024) --- held that scraping for training-dataset construction
can fall under \S{}~44b. The judgment is one early data point;
appellate review, the related EU AI Act (in force August 2024)
Art.~53 transparency-and-copyright obligations on general-purpose AI
providers, and ongoing Commission guidance will reshape the picture.
All sources here are \texttt{[unverified-external]} in
\texttt{docs/sources.md} until a targeted German-language search reads
each primary text. \emph{(iii) Why this matters for our paper.} Two
consequences follow. First, the AI assistant is correctly acknowledged
in \cref{sec:ai-disclosure-models} but is not a co-author; the human
author holds copyright. Second, the choice of CC-BY-4.0 (see
\texttt{LICENSE}) attaches to the human-authored and human-curated
portions of the work; it does not purport to license the AI's training
data, the AI model itself, or vendor artifacts vendored under

repository is mirrored to a journal or to Zenodo.}
The reason is technical and legal in equal measure: under \S{}~2 UrhG
(German Copyright Act, \emph{Urheberrechtsgesetz}), copyright
protection requires a \emph{pers\"onliche geistige Sch\"opfung} --- a
personal intellectual creation by a human --- so an AI model cannot
hold copyright in its outputs, cannot consent to publication, and
cannot be held accountable in the way that academic authorship
implies. Editorial responsibility for every paragraph rests with the

\section{The Pandora moment: this paper as a novel mode of transparent AI-assisted research}
\label{sec:pandora}

\begin{quote}
\emph{``Only Hope remained there in an unbreakable home within under
the rim of the great jar, and did not fly out at the door.''}\\
\hfill --- Hesiod, \emph{Works and Days}, ll.~96--98
(trans.\ Hugh G.\ Evelyn-White, 1914)
\end{quote}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{figures/logo-pandora-jar-intact.png}
\Description{Stylised illustration of an intact ceramic jar, the Hesiodic counterpoint to the shattered-jar logo in the front matter.}

\label{fig:pandora-jar-intact}

Pandora's jar is open. Generative AI is now a routine part of how
scientific manuscripts are drafted, and the empirical literature
documents what comes out of the jar: fabricated citation rates of
18\%--55\% for raw GPT outputs~\citep{walters2023fabricated},
hallucination rates up to 91.4\% in systematic-review reference
generation~\citep{chelli2024hallucination}, paper-mill output growing
from 4 to 190 NHANES papers in a single
year~\citep{suchak2025nhanes}, and a longer-horizon model-collapse
risk in which the next generation of foundation models is trained on
the corrupted outputs of the present
one~\citep{shumailov2024modelcollapse, seddik2024collapse}. These
problems are not solved by refusing to use AI; refusal does not put the
technology back in the jar, and it concedes the ground of methodological
practice to those who \emph{do} use it without disclosure. What is left
in the jar, on the Hesiodic reading, is \emph{Hope} --- but the kind of
Hope that demands work.

The novelty we claim in this paper is not in the substance of the case
studies. Spider Farmer's BLE protocol and EcoFlow's API surfaces are
prior community work; the meta-process is recursive by construction.
The novelty is in \emph{how} the case studies are written. Eight
integrated practices distinguish this paper from the prevailing modes
of AI-assisted research:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{AI conversation transcripts as first-class artifacts.}
        Every paper-development session is preserved in
        \seqsplit{\texttt{experiments/*/raw\_conversations}} \seqsplit{\texttt{(copy\&paste,~web)/}}
        with an explicit verification status
        (\texttt{[verbatim-export]} /
        \texttt{[curated-reconstruction]} / \texttt{[redacted]}). The
        transcript is not appendix material --- it is the lab notebook
        of the LLM era. Readers can audit which arguments came from
        which prompt.
  \item \textbf{A verification-status legend for every cited source.}
        The labels \texttt{[repo-vendored]},
        \texttt{[repo-referenced]}, \texttt{[unverified-external]},
        \texttt{[lit-retrieved]}, \texttt{[lit-read]}, and
        \texttt{[needs-research]} make the read-state of every
        citation explicit. The discipline is mundane --- never upgrade
        an entry without reading the full text --- and the mundanity
        is the point.
  \item \textbf{Provenance maps tying every technical claim to
        commit-pinned evidence.}
        \texttt{experiments/*/provenance.md} maps each transcript
        decision to specific files and line numbers in the embedded
        vendor code (commit \texttt{ffdf60c}).
  \item \textbf{Mirror discipline between prose and submission source.}
        \texttt{paper/main.md} and \texttt{paper/main.tex} are kept
        consistent at every commit (rule 11 in
        \texttt{CLAUDE\_CODE\_INSTRUCTIONS.md}); CI rebuilds the PDF on
        every paper-touching commit and surfaces regressions.
  \item \textbf{A recursive case study of the paper itself
        (\cref{sec:case-meta}).} The paper-generation pipeline is
        treated as a third reverse-engineering case --- same threat
        model, same artifact inventory, same dual-use evaluation. A
        methodology that cannot describe its own production is not a
        methodology, it is a brand.
  \item \textbf{Explicit AI disclosure framed against empirical base
        rates.} \cref{sec:ai-disclosure} names the model, the division
        of labour, and the empirical fabrication rates the methodology
        is responding to. The disclosure is not an apology --- it is
        the unit of accountability that lets the work be audited rather
        than guessed at.
  \item \textbf{Legal honesty about authorship.} The footnote on
        \emph{Urheberrecht und KI} in
        \cref{sec:ai-disclosure-models} explains why, under
        \S{}~2 UrhG, the AI is acknowledged as a contributor but is
        not and cannot be a co-author, and what that means for the
        CC-BY-4.0 grant.
  \item \textbf{FAIR alignment as a precondition, not an afterthought.}
        \texttt{CITATION.cff}, \texttt{.zenodo.json},
        \texttt{codemeta.json}, and \texttt{docs/fair.md} map every
        FAIR principle to the concrete repository feature that
        satisfies it.
\end{enumerate}

A \emph{ninth} practice has come into focus during this paper's own
preparation, and is logged here for the next iteration of the
framework rather than added to \cref{fig:eight-practices}. It
addresses a meta-question that the eight practices above do not
directly answer --- \emph{how do we safeguard against unintentional
plagiarism?} The verification ladder (practice 2) and provenance maps
(practice 3) make every cited claim auditable, and the
transcript-as-artifact discipline (practice 1) makes the
prompt-to-paragraph link explicit; what they do \emph{not} directly
catch is a generation-time risk: that an LLM might reproduce passages
from its training data closely enough to constitute plagiarism even
when neither the human author nor the model intends it.

\begin{enumerate}
  \setcounter{enumi}{8}
  \item \textbf{Verbatim-string scanning against the assistant's
        likely training corpus.} The mitigation operationalised in
        this paper is mundane and partial: every paper-ready paragraph
        is searched against the public web (Exa MCP
        \texttt{web\_search\_exa}) for suspicious verbatim $n$-gram
        hits before commit; matches above a threshold are either
        replaced with paraphrase plus citation, or wrapped in an
        inline quotation with attribution. This is a \emph{first cut}.
        It does not catch paraphrase-level overlap (which requires
        similarity tooling beyond exact-string search), and it does
        not catch overlap with closed corpora the assistant was
        trained on. The honest framing is that the plagiarism-safeguard
        problem in the AI-assisted-writing era is not yet solved, and
        that practice~9 will need to evolve with detection tooling as
        that tooling matures. It is named here so that the \emph{next}
        iteration of this framework can argue with it, replace it, or
        tighten it (Author's Note: \emph{Frameworks evolve with
        capabilities}).
\end{enumerate}

None of these practices is individually novel. Conversation logs have
been shipped with replications before; verification-status labels
exist in evidence-based-medicine practice; provenance maps exist in
bioinformatics; FAIR predates LLMs. The closest published comparators
address the same problem at three different layers: at the
publishing-system layer, Sabel \& Larhammar's \emph{Stockholm
Declaration} (L-SLOP-7) proposes academia-controlled non-profit
publishing, quality-not-quantity incentives, and independent fraud
detection; at the practitioner layer, Cheng, Calhoun \& Reedy
(L-SLOP-10) articulate four key principles and three categories of
ethically sound generative-AI use for academic writing; at the
technical-mitigation layer, Pellegrina \& Helmy (L-SLOP-12) review
AI detectors and AI-assisted error / citation / image-verification
tooling and conclude that these ``are not yet sufficiently accurate or
reliable yet for use in academic assessment''. The differential of the
present work is to apply these three strands in concert to the
security-research interoperability context, and to operationalise them
as an \emph{executable agent prompt}
(\texttt{docs/prompts/research-protocol-prompt.md}) that an AI agent
can be instructed to follow and that a human reviewer can audit by
reading the artifacts the protocol produces. We do not claim to
subsume the Stockholm Declaration's system-level reforms or
Cheng~et~al.'s practitioner guidelines; we claim to extend them into a
runnable protocol whose compliance is checkable per artifact.

\paragraph{A real-world precedent for the redaction discipline.}
Two of the eight practices --- provenance maps (practice~3) and FAIR
alignment (practice~8) --- sit alongside an unlisted hygiene step
that this paper enforces under \texttt{CLAUDE.md} rules 12 and 13:
secrets, personal data, and other rule-12 material must be redacted
\emph{and} purged from git history before any public-facing release,
because \texttt{git rm} alone leaves the deleted blobs reachable from
prior commits. A recently-dated, publicly-visible enactment of
exactly this discipline appeared during this paper's preparation: on
2026-05-03 the upstream \texttt{noheton/powerocean-dev} integration
removed its \texttt{doc/} subtree (APK splits, derivative APK
analysis, vendor PDFs, vendor sample-code archive,
GDPR-personal-data files, raw extraction logs) on UrhG / GDPR
grounds and replaced it with an 80-line \texttt{doc/README.md} that
explicitly notes ``\texttt{git rm} does not purge history'' and
points readers to \texttt{git filter-repo} on a fresh clone for a
true
purge~\citep{noheton2026powerocean5c8b815,noheton2026powerocean_docreadme}.
The precedent strengthens the methodological argument made here
without requiring this paper to claim attribution: an independent
AI-assisted-integration project, working under separate constraints,
arrived at the same redaction-and-history-rewrite step that the
paper's rule-12 / rule-13 framing demands.

\paragraph{A fourth structural claim: democratisation of science production.}
The effort-gap collapse documented in
\cref{sec:case-spider-farmer} and \cref{sec:case-ecoflow} is not
confined to device integration. The meta-process
(\cref{sec:case-meta}) is the same phenomenon applied recursively to
the \emph{production of empirical research itself}: a single
independent researcher, without institutional infrastructure,
produced a publication-quality paper---provenance maps, FAIR
metadata, dual-format submission source, 70-entry literature
register---in approximately 17 hours of AI-assisted work, estimated
at 6\% of the manual-equivalent effort
(\cref{sec:meta-kpis}). This is citizen science, but in a mode that
has no direct precedent in the citizen-science tradition.

Classical citizen science democratises \emph{data collection}:
Galaxy~Zoo enlists amateur observers to classify galaxies;
Folding@home distributes protein-folding computation; iNaturalist
aggregates biodiversity observations. The bottleneck those platforms
attack is human time at the sensing layer; research design and
analysis remain institutional. What AI-assisted research
democratises is different---it attacks the \emph{cognitive and
documentational} bottleneck: the activation energy of turning a
well-posed question and working integration evidence into a
structured, attributed, reproducible argument. That bottleneck has
historically been managed by institutional infrastructure: research
libraries, writing support, supervisory relationships, peer networks,
methodology courses. Those barriers are not gone; they are lower.

This is both the most hopeful and the most dangerous implication of
the present work. Hopeful, because it extends the reach of the
scientific method to researchers who lack institutional affiliation,
funding, or time---groups that include the fastest-growing category
of knowledge workers in the current economy. Dangerous, because the
same compression applies to low-quality production as to high-quality
production; \cref{sec:disc-sloppification} and
\cref{sec:disc-model-collapse} are the corpus-level consequence of
democratised \emph{production} without democratised
\emph{verification}. The response is not to restrict access to the
tools but to make the methodology auditable---to ensure that, as the
jar opens wider, the Hope that remains is the kind that shows its
work.

The implicit contrast is with the prevailing practice. Two failure
modes dominate the AI-assisted research literature today, and both
are documented quantitatively in \texttt{docs/sources.md} cluster I:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Concealment.} AI is used heavily but not disclosed;
        the read-state of every citation is indeterminate. The
        fabricated-citation literature (L-SLOP-1, L-SLOP-2,
        L-SLOP-4) is what concealment produces at scale.
  \item \textbf{Token disclosure.} A generic ``AI was used to assist''
        sentence in the acknowledgements with no mapping from sentence
        to commit, no transcript, and no verification-status
        discipline. This satisfies a publisher's checkbox but is not
        auditable.
\end{itemize}
We argue that a third mode is now necessary and feasible:
\textbf{artifact-level disclosure}, in which every AI-assisted
decision is traceable to a transcript, a commit, and a verification
status. The cost is real --- this paper's repository carries roughly
as many lines of methodological scaffolding as paper prose --- but it
is paid in labour, not in lost capability. Sloppification
(\cref{sec:disc-sloppification}) and model collapse
(\cref{sec:disc-model-collapse}) are each a tax on the scientific
commons; artifact-level disclosure is the principal means we have to
refuse to pay either tax.

Pandora's box is open. The Hope that remains is the kind that does
work.
