\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates how modern large language models collapse the traditional
``effort gap'' in reverse engineering. Through two case studies, we show how
AI-assisted analysis turns theoretical protocol knowledge into practical,
replicable local integrations, and we assess whether this is a force for
interoperability or a systemic security risk.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
The rise of proprietary IoT ecosystems has made local interoperability difficult
by design. This study asks: \emph{is AI-assisted hacking a key to
interoperability, or does it turn security through obscurity into a security
nightmare?} We argue that the central barrier is not a lack of technical
capability but the activation energy required to move from theory to practice.
With AI, that barrier is shrinking, and the implications are both liberating
and dangerous.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We define the ``effort gap'' in modern IoT reverse engineering.
  \item We present two empirical case studies: Spider Farmer and EcoFlow PowerOcean.
  \item We propose a methodology for auditing AI-assisted research, including
        artifact analysis, documentation comparison, and commit-history review.
  \item We evaluate interoperability benefits against dual-use security risks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Data acquisition:} APKs, firmware, packet captures, documentation,
        and prompt logs.
  \item \textbf{AI-assisted analysis:} combining generated and existing
        documentation with code and protocol inspection, including exported
        conversation transcripts as research artifacts.
  \item \textbf{Version-history review:} commit and branch analysis to understand
        development evolution and research provenance.
  \item \textbf{Validation:} reproducing findings and documenting failed attempts
        in line with \citet{dfg2023}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion: Interoperability vs.\ Security Nightmare}
\label{sec:discussion}
We discuss the democratization of reverse engineering and the new ``boredom
barrier''; consumer sovereignty, right-to-repair, and local data ownership;
dual-use risks including vendor attack surface, rapid protocol discovery, and
abuse potential; and the future of IoT security through open APIs, zero-trust,
and accountable design.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
AI-assisted hacking is a double-edged force: it enables local interoperability
and user freedom while exposing the fragility of security through obscurity.
The path forward is not to ban AI, but to design systems that do not depend on
secrecy.

\section*{Future Work}
Expand empirical evaluation to additional devices and vendors; develop
reproducible prompt templates and audit workflows; propose a responsible
disclosure framework for AI-assisted reverse engineering.
